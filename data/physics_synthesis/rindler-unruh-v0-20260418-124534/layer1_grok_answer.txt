**Part 1: Derivation of Rindler Coordinates**

Consider Minkowski spacetime in 1+1 dimensions with metric signature \((+,-)\):
\[
ds^2 = dt^2 - dx^2.
\]
An observer with constant proper acceleration \(a > 0\) (in the \(+x\) direction) follows a worldline \(x^\mu(\tau)\) parameterized by proper time \(\tau\), where the 4-velocity \(u^\mu = dx^\mu/d\tau\) satisfies
\[
u^\mu u_\mu = 1, \qquad a^\mu = \frac{Du^\mu}{d\tau}, \qquad a^\mu u_\mu = 0,
\]
and the proper acceleration scalar is \(\alpha = \sqrt{-a^\mu a_\mu} = a\) (constant).

Introduce the rapidity \(\phi(\tau)\) via
\[
\frac{dt}{d\tau} = \cosh\phi, \qquad \frac{dx}{d\tau} = \sinh\phi.
\]
This automatically satisfies \(u^\mu u_\mu = 1\). Differentiating and imposing constant proper acceleration yields \(d\phi/d\tau = a\), so (choosing \(\phi(0) = 0\))
\[
\phi = a\tau.
\]
Integration gives the worldline
\[
t(\tau) = \frac{1}{a}\sinh(a\tau), \qquad x(\tau) = \frac{1}{a}\cosh(a\tau).
\]
Eliminating \(\tau\) produces
\[
x^2 - t^2 = \frac{1}{a^2},
\]
which is a hyperbola in Minkowski coordinates (one branch with \(x > |t|\)).

To generalize to a coordinate system covering the right Rindler wedge (\(x > |t|\)), introduce Rindler coordinates \((\eta, \xi)\) via the explicit transformation
\[
t = \frac{1}{a} e^{a\xi} \sinh(a\eta), \qquad x = \frac{1}{a} e^{a\xi} \cosh(a\eta),
\]
where \(\eta \in (-\infty, +\infty)\) and \(\xi \in (-\infty, +\infty)\). (Transverse coordinates \(y,z\) are unchanged.) At fixed \(\xi = 0\), this reduces exactly to the hyperbolic worldline above with proper time \(\tau = \eta\).

Invert the transformation:
\[
\eta = \frac{1}{2a}\ln\left(\frac{x+t}{x-t}\right), \qquad \xi = \frac{1}{2a}\ln[a^2(x^2 - t^2)].
\]
To obtain the metric in Rindler coordinates, compute the differentials:
\[
dt = e^{a\xi}\bigl[\sinh(a\eta)\,d\xi + \cosh(a\eta)\,d\eta\bigr], \qquad dx = e^{a\xi}\bigl[\cosh(a\eta)\,d\xi + \sinh(a\eta)\,d\eta\bigr].
\]
Substitute into \(ds^2 = dt^2 - dx^2\):
\[
dt^2 - dx^2 = e^{2a\xi}(d\eta^2 - d\xi^2).
\]
Thus (restoring transverse directions)
\[
ds^2 = e^{2a\xi}(d\eta^2 - d\xi^2) - dy^2 - dz^2.
\]
For an observer at constant \(\xi\), \(d\xi = dy = dz = 0\), so \(ds^2 = e^{2a\xi}\,d\eta^2\). The proper time is therefore \(d\tau = e^{a\xi}\,d\eta\). The 4-acceleration magnitude measured by this observer is \(a\,e^{-a\xi}\); in particular, the observer at \(\xi = 0\) has proper acceleration exactly \(a\).

The Rindler horizon is the surface \(\xi \to -\infty\). In Rindler coordinates this appears as a coordinate singularity: \(g_{\eta\eta} = e^{2a\xi} \to 0\) while all curvature invariants remain zero (the spacetime is flat). To see that it is a null surface in Minkowski coordinates, note that
\[
t + x = a^{-1}\exp[a(\eta + \xi)], \qquad t - x = -a^{-1}\exp[a(\xi - \eta)].
\]
As \(\xi \to -\infty\) at fixed finite \(\eta\), both \(t+x \to 0\) and \(t-x \to 0\), i.e., the surface approaches the lightlike lines \(x = \pm t\) (null because \(ds^2 = 0\) along \(dt = \pm dx\)). These are event horizons for the Rindler observers: signals from beyond \(\xi = -\infty\) can never reach them. The coordinate singularity is removable by returning to Minkowski coordinates, where the geometry is regular across \(x = |t|\) (but the wedges \(\xi > -\infty\) and \(\xi < -\infty\) correspond to causally disconnected regions).

**Part 2: Derivation of the Unruh Temperature**

Consider a massless scalar field \(\phi\) obeying the wave equation \(\square\phi = 0\). In Minkowski coordinates the field admits a mode expansion in plane-wave basis functions that are positive-frequency with respect to Minkowski time \(t\):
\[
\phi(x) = \int_0^\infty\frac{d\omega}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega}}\Bigl[a_{\omega} e^{-i\omega t + ikx} + a_{\omega}^\dagger e^{i\omega t - ikx}\Bigr]
\]
(with appropriate transverse momentum integrals; we work in 1+1 dimensions for transparency). The Minkowski vacuum \(|0_M\rangle\) is defined by
\[
a_{\omega}|0_M\rangle = 0 \quad \forall\,\omega > 0.
\]
The associated Fock states define Minkowski particles.

A uniformly accelerating observer uses proper time \(\tau \propto \eta\) and therefore regards modes that are positive-frequency with respect to Rindler time \(\eta\) as defining his particles. The Rindler mode functions (right wedge) satisfying the wave equation and positive-frequency condition \(\partial_\eta u_\omega^R \propto -i\omega u_\omega^R\) (\(\omega > 0\)) are
\[
u_\omega^R(\eta,\xi) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega}}\,e^{-i\omega\eta} f(\xi),
\]
where \(f(\xi)\) solves the spatial equation (for massless fields the dependence is on the null coordinates \(U = t+x \propto e^{a(\eta+\xi)}\) and \(V = t-x\)). The field admits an alternative expansion
\[
\phi(x) = \int_0^\infty\frac{d\omega}{\sqrt{4\pi\omega}}\Bigl[b_{\omega} u_\omega^R + b_{\omega}^\dagger (u_\omega^R)^* + \text{left-wedge terms}\Bigr],
\]
with associated vacuum \(|0_R\rangle\) annihilated by all \(b_\omega\).

The two complete orthonormal bases \(\{u_\omega^M\}\) and \(\{u_\omega^R\}\) are linearly related by a Bogoliubov transformation (key physical assumption: the field operator \(\phi\) is unique and can be expanded in either basis; the transformation coefficients are determined by the Klein-Gordon inner product on a Cauchy surface):
\[
u_\omega^M = \int_0^\infty d\omega'\Bigl(\alpha_{\omega\omega'} u_{\omega'}^R + \beta_{\omega\omega'} (u_{\omega'}^R)^*\Bigr).
\]
The inverse transformation expresses the Rindler operators in terms of Minkowski ones:
\[
b_\omega = \int_0^\infty d\omega'\Bigl(\alpha_{\omega\omega'}^* a_{\omega'} - \beta_{\omega\omega'}^* a_{\omega'}^\dagger\Bigr).
\]
(The left-wedge modes must be included to obtain a complete basis across the full spacetime; they mix with the right-wedge modes through the horizon.)

The Minkowski vacuum is therefore not the Rindler vacuum. The expectation value of the Rindler number operator in the Minkowski vacuum is
\[
\langle N_\omega\rangle \equiv {}_M\langle 0|b_\omega^\dagger b_\omega|0\rangle_M = \int_0^\infty d\omega'\,|\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2.
\]
Explicit evaluation of the overlap integrals (using the relation \(U \propto e^{a\eta}\) between null coordinates and performing a contour integration or invoking the Gamma-function identity \(\Gamma(1+ix)\Gamma(-ix) = 2\pi/(e^{2\pi x}-1)\)) yields
\[
|\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2 = \delta(\omega - \omega')\frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/a}-1}.
\]
Consequently
\[
\langle N_\omega\rangle = \frac{1}{e^{2\pi\omega/a}-1}.
\]
This is precisely a Bose-Einstein thermal distribution
\[
\langle N_\omega\rangle = \frac{1}{e^{\omega/T}-1}
\]
with Unruh temperature (restoring \(\hbar, c, k_B\))
\[
T = \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi c k_B}.
\]
(The factor \(2\pi\) originates from the periodicity in imaginary Rindler time \(\eta \to \eta + 2\pi i/a\) that makes the Minkowski vacuum appear thermal to the accelerated observer.) The same result follows from the reduced density matrix obtained by tracing over the left wedge: \(\rho_R = \operatorname{Tr}_L|0_M\rangle\langle 0_M|\) is thermal at the Unruh temperature.

All steps up to the explicit form of \(|\beta_{\omega\omega'}|^2\) are exact. The overlap integral itself is a standard but technical result (invoking analytic continuation across the Rindler horizon); it is invoked here without performing the contour integration explicitly, as is conventional in derivations of the Unruh effect.
