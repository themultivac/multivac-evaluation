# Part 1: Derivation of Rindler Coordinates

## Hyperbolic Motion of a Uniformly Accelerating Observer

We work in (1+1)-dimensional Minkowski spacetime with metric signature \((+,-)\):
\[
ds^2 = dt^2 - dx^2.
\]
An observer undergoing constant proper acceleration \(a\) has a worldline parametrized by proper time \(\tau\). The four-velocity \(u^\mu = (dt/d\tau, dx/d\tau)\) satisfies
\[
u^\mu u_\mu = \left(\frac{dt}{d\tau}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{dx}{d\tau}\right)^2 = 1.
\]
The four-acceleration \(a^\mu = du^\mu/d\tau\) must satisfy
\[
a^\mu a_\mu = -a^2,
\]
and orthogonality \(u^\mu a_\mu = 0\). Assuming motion along the \(x\)-axis and that the observer is momentarily at rest at \(t=0\) with \(x = 1/a\), we solve the equations. From orthogonality,
\[
\frac{dt}{d\tau} \frac{d^2 t}{d\tau^2} - \frac{dx}{d\tau} \frac{d^2 x}{d\tau^2} = 0.
\]
Using the constant proper acceleration condition, one finds
\[
\frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{v}{\sqrt{1-v^2}} \right) = a, \quad v = \frac{dx}{dt}.
\]
Integrating with \(v=0\) at \(t=0\) gives
\[
\frac{v}{\sqrt{1-v^2}} = a t \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = \frac{a t}{\sqrt{1+a^2 t^2}}.
\]
Then \(\gamma = dt/d\tau = \sqrt{1+a^2 t^2}\). Integrating \(dx/dt = v\) with \(x(0)=1/a\) yields
\[
x(t) = \frac{1}{a} \sqrt{1+a^2 t^2}.
\]
Thus the worldline is a hyperbola:
\[
x^2 - t^2 = \frac{1}{a^2}.
\]

## Coordinate Transformation to Rindler Coordinates

We wish to introduce coordinates \((\eta, \xi)\) that cover the right Rindler wedge \(x > |t|\). Let
\[
t = \xi \sinh \eta, \quad x = \xi \cosh \eta, \quad \xi > 0, \quad -\infty < \eta < \infty.
\]
Then
\[
x^2 - t^2 = \xi^2 (\cosh^2 \eta - \sinh^2 \eta) = \xi^2.
\]
The observer's hyperbola \(x^2 - t^2 = 1/a^2\) corresponds to \(\xi = 1/a = \text{constant}\). The proper time along this worldline is given by \(d\tau = \xi d\eta = d\eta / a\), so \(\eta = a \tau\) (up to an additive constant).

The metric in these coordinates is computed:
\[
\begin{aligned}
dt &= \sinh \eta \, d\xi + \xi \cosh \eta \, d\eta, \\
dx &= \cosh \eta \, d\xi + \xi \sinh \eta \, d\eta, \\
ds^2 &= dt^2 - dx^2 \\
&= (\sinh \eta \, d\xi + \xi \cosh \eta \, d\eta)^2 - (\cosh \eta \, d\xi + \xi \sinh \eta \, d\eta)^2 \\
&= (\sinh^2 \eta - \cosh^2 \eta) d\xi^2 + 2\xi(\sinh \eta \cosh \eta - \cosh \eta \sinh \eta) d\xi d\eta + \xi^2(\cosh^2 \eta - \sinh^2 \eta) d\eta^2 \\
&= -\ d\xi^2 + \xi^2 d\eta^2.
\end{aligned}
\]
Thus the Rindler metric is
\[
ds^2 = \xi^2 d\eta^2 - d\xi^2.
\]
An observer at constant \(\xi\) has proper acceleration \(\alpha = 1/\xi\). For \(\xi = 1/a\), we recover \(\alpha = a\).

## Rindler Horizon

The lines \(t = \pm x\) (with \(x>0\)) bound the right Rindler wedge. In Rindler coordinates, these correspond to \(\xi = 0\) and \(\eta \to \pm \infty\). Indeed, as \(\xi \to 0\) with finite \(\eta\), we approach the origin \((t,x)=(0,0)\), but the lines \(t = \pm x\) are reached by taking \(\eta \to \pm \infty\) while \(\xi \to 0\) such that \(t/x = \tanh \eta \to \pm 1\). The metric component \(g_{\eta\eta} = \xi^2\) vanishes at \(\xi=0\), making it a coordinate singularity. In Minkowski coordinates, along \(t = \pm x\), we have \(ds^2 = 0\), so these are null surfaces. They constitute the Rindler horizon, a Killing horizon associated with the boost Killing vector \(\partial_\eta\).

# Part 2: Derivation of the Unruh Temperature

## Setup: Massless Scalar Field

Consider a free massless scalar field \(\phi\) in (1+1)-dimensional Minkowski spacetime. The field equation is the wave equation:
\[
\partial_t^2 \phi - \partial_x^2 \phi = 0.
\]
We use units where \(\hbar = c = k_B = 1\) for simplicity; the final result will restore constants.

### Minkowski Mode Expansion

The field can be expanded in plane-wave modes:
\[
\phi = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dk \left( a_k u_k + a_k^\dagger u_k^* \right),
\]
where the positive-frequency modes (with respect to Minkowski time \(t\)) are
\[
u_k = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi |k|}} e^{-i|k| t + i k x}.
\]
These satisfy \((u_k, u_{k'}) = \delta(k-k')\) with the Klein-Gordon inner product
\[
(\phi_1, \phi_2) = i \int dx \left( \phi_1^* \partial_t \phi_2 - (\partial_t \phi_1^*) \phi_2 \right).
\]
The Minkowski vacuum \(|0_M\rangle\) is defined by \(a_k |0_M\rangle = 0\) for all \(k\).

### Rindler Mode Expansion

In the right Rindler wedge, we use coordinates \((\eta, \xi)\) with metric \(ds^2 = \xi^2 d\eta^2 - d\xi^2\). The field equation becomes
\[
\partial_\eta^2 \phi = \xi^2 \partial_\xi^2 \phi + \xi \partial_\xi \phi.
\]
We seek positive-frequency modes with respect to Rindler time \(\eta\). For convenience, we work with light-cone coordinates. Define:
\[
U = t - x, \quad V = t + x.
\]
In the right wedge, \(U < 0\), \(V > 0\). The transformation to Rindler coordinates gives
\[
U = -\xi e^{-\eta}, \quad V = \xi e^{\eta}.
\]
A right-moving mode depends only on \(U\). A positive-frequency Rindler mode in the right wedge is of the form
\[
v_{\Omega}^{(R)} = N_\Omega \theta(-U) (-U)^{i\Omega} = N_\Omega \theta(-U) e^{i\Omega \ln(-U)},
\]
which, when expressed in \((\eta,\xi)\), becomes \(v_{\Omega}^{(R)} = N_\Omega e^{-i\Omega \eta} \xi^{i\Omega}\) (up to a phase). Similarly, for the left wedge (where \(U>0\), \(V<0\)), we define positive-frequency modes
\[
v_{\Omega}^{(L)} = N_\Omega \theta(V) V^{-i\Omega}.
\]
These modes are orthogonal and complete in their respective wedges. The normalization constant is chosen as
\[
N_\Omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi \Omega}} \quad \text{or similar, but precise value is not crucial.}
\]
The field expansion in terms of Rindler modes is
\[
\phi = \int_0^\infty d\Omega \left( b_{\Omega}^{(R)} v_{\Omega}^{(R)} + b_{\Omega}^{(R)\dagger} v_{\Omega}^{(R)*} + b_{\Omega}^{(L)} v_{\Omega}^{(L)} + b_{\Omega}^{(L)\dagger} v_{\Omega}^{(L)*} \right),
\]
where \(b_{\Omega}^{(R)}\) and \(b_{\Omega}^{(L)}\) annihilate the Rindler vacua in the right and left wedges, respectively.

### Bogoliubov Transformation

The Minkowski and Rindler mode expansions represent the same field, so the operators are related by a Bogoliubov transformation. We compute the transformation coefficients by taking inner products between Minkowski and Rindler modes. For a right-moving Minkowski mode \(u_\omega \propto e^{-i\omega U}\) (with \(\omega>0\)), we express it as an integral over Rindler modes:
\[
e^{-i\omega U} = \int_0^\infty d\Omega \left( \alpha_{\omega\Omega} v_{\Omega}^{(R)} + \beta_{\omega\Omega} v_{\Omega}^{(R)*} + \text{(left wedge contributions)} \right).
\]
Because \(e^{-i\omega U}\) is analytic in the lower half of the complex \(U\)-plane (since \(\omega>0\)), and the Rindler modes have specific analyticity properties, the Bogoliubov coefficients can be computed via integrals. Specifically, for \(U<0\),
\[
e^{-i\omega U} = \int_0^\infty \frac{d\Omega}{2\pi} \left( \alpha_{\omega\Omega} (-U)^{i\Omega} + \beta_{\omega\Omega} (-U)^{-i\Omega} \right).
\]
This is essentially a Mellin transform. Using the integral representation of the Gamma function,
\[
\int_0^\infty d\Omega \, \Omega^{i\Omega - 1} e^{-i\omega U} \text{? Actually, standard result:}
\]
We compute \(\alpha_{\omega\Omega}\) and \(\beta_{\omega\Omega}\) by evaluating
\[
\alpha_{\omega\Omega} = (v_{\Omega}^{(R)}, u_\omega), \quad \beta_{\omega\Omega} = - (v_{\Omega}^{(R)*}, u_\omega).
\]
After careful calculation (see, e.g., Birrell & Davies or Crispino et al., Rev. Mod. Phys. 80, 787 (2008)), one finds
\[
\alpha_{\omega\Omega} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi a \omega}} e^{\pi \Omega/(2a)} \left( \frac{\omega}{a} \right)^{i\Omega/a} \Gamma\left(-\frac{i\Omega}{a}\right),
\]
\[
\beta_{\omega\Omega} = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi a \omega}} e^{-\pi \Omega/(2a)} \left( \frac{\omega}{a} \right)^{i\Omega/a} \Gamma\left(-\frac{i\Omega}{a}\right).
\]
The ratio satisfies
\[
\left| \frac{\beta_{\omega\Omega}}{\alpha_{\omega\Omega}} \right| = e^{-\pi \Omega/a}.
\]

### Thermal Spectrum

The transformation between annihilation and creation operators takes the form (for each \(\Omega\))
\[
a_\omega = \int_0^\infty d\Omega \left( \alpha_{\omega\Omega} b_{\Omega}^{(R)} + \beta_{\omega\Omega}^* b_{\Omega}^{(R)\dagger} + \text{(left terms)} \right).
\]
However, a simpler derivation considers the two-mode squeezing structure. One finds that the Minkowski vacuum can be expressed as an entangled state of Rindler particles:
\[
|0_M\rangle = \prod_\Omega \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - e^{-2\pi \Omega/a}}} \exp\left( e^{-\pi \Omega/a} b_{\Omega}^{(R)\dagger} b_{\Omega}^{(L)\dagger} \right) |0_R\rangle \otimes |0_L\rangle,
\]
where \(|0_R\rangle\) and \(|0_L\rangle\) are the Rindler vacua in the right and left wedges. Tracing over the left wedge degrees of freedom yields a thermal density matrix for the right wedge:
\[
\rho_R = \operatorname{Tr}_L |0_M\rangle \langle 0_M| = \prod_\Omega \left(1 - e^{-2\pi \Omega/a}\right) \sum_{n=0}^\infty e^{-2\pi n \Omega/a} |n_\Omega\rangle \langle n_\Omega|,
\]
where \(|n_\Omega\rangle\) are the \(n\)-particle states for mode \(\Omega\) in the right wedge. This is a thermal Bose–Einstein distribution with temperature
\[
T = \frac{a}{2\pi}.
\]
Restoring constants \(\hbar\), \(c\), and \(k_B\), we note that \(a\) has dimensions of acceleration, and the combination \(\hbar a/(c k_B)\) has dimensions of temperature. Since our derivation gave \(T = a/(2\pi)\), the correct expression is
\[
T = \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi c k_B}.
\]
This is the Unruh temperature.

## Key Physical Assumption

The derivation relies on the Bogoliubov transformation between Minkowski and Rindler mode decompositions. The essential physical assumption is that the Minkowski vacuum state is the physically relevant state for inertial observers, and that quantum field theory in curved spacetime (here, the Rindler metric) can be constructed by defining positive-frequency modes with respect to the appropriate time coordinate (here, Rindler time \(\eta\)). The thermal spectrum emerges because the notions of positive frequency differ between inertial and accelerating observers, leading to particle production in the accelerated frame.

---

**Final Result:** An observer with constant proper acceleration \(a\) in Minkowski vacuum perceives a thermal bath of particles at temperature \(T = \frac{\hbar a}{2\pi c k_B}\).
