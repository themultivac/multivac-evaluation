Here is the complete derivation of the Rindler coordinates and the Unruh effect. 

### Part 1: Rindler Coordinates from First Principles

**1. Setup and Assumptions**
We work in 1+1 dimensional Minkowski spacetime with coordinates $(t, x)$ and signature $(+, -, -, -)$. The metric is:
$$ ds^2 = c^2 dt^2 - dx^2 $$
Consider an observer moving with constant proper acceleration $a$. Let $\tau$ be the observer's proper time. The 4-velocity is $u^\mu = (c \frac{dt}{d\tau}, \frac{dx}{d\tau})$ and the 4-acceleration is $a^\mu = \frac{du^\mu}{d\tau}$. 
The physical constraints are:
1. Normalization of 4-velocity: $u_\mu u^\mu = c^2$
2. Orthogonality of velocity and acceleration: $u_\mu a^\mu = 0$
3. Constant proper acceleration magnitude: $a_\mu a^\mu = -a^2$

**2. Deriving the Worldline**
We can parameterize the 4-velocity using a rapidity parameter $\theta(\tau)$:
$$ u^\mu = (c \cosh \theta, c \sinh \theta) $$
This automatically satisfies $u_\mu u^\mu = c^2 (\cosh^2\theta - \sinh^2\theta) = c^2$. 
Taking the derivative with respect to proper time $\tau$ gives the 4-acceleration:
$$ a^\mu = \frac{du^\mu}{d\tau} = \left( c \sinh \theta \frac{d\theta}{d\tau}, c \cosh \theta \frac{d\theta}{d\tau} \right) $$
Applying the constant acceleration constraint $a_\mu a^\mu = -a^2$:
$$ c^2 \sinh^2\theta \left(\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}\right)^2 - c^2 \cosh^2\theta \left(\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}\right)^2 = -a^2 \implies -c^2 \left(\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}\right)^2 = -a^2 $$
Assuming the observer accelerates in the positive $x$ direction, we integrate $\frac{d\theta}{d\tau} = \frac{a}{c}$ with initial condition $\theta(0)=0$ to find $\theta(\tau) = \frac{a\tau}{c}$.

Now, we integrate the 4-velocity to find the coordinates $(t, x)$:
$$ c \frac{dt}{d\tau} = c \cosh\left(\frac{a\tau}{c}\right) \implies t(\tau) = \frac{c}{a} \sinh\left(\frac{a\tau}{c}\right) $$
$$ \frac{dx}{d\tau} = c \sinh\left(\frac{a\tau}{c}\right) \implies x(\tau) = \frac{c^2}{a} \cosh\left(\frac{a\tau}{c}\right) $$
(We chose integration constants such that at $\tau=0$, $t=0$ and $x=c^2/a$). 

**3. The Hyperbola**
To find the trajectory in Minkowski space, we eliminate $\tau$:
$$ x^2 - c^2 t^2 = \frac{c^4}{a^2} \left[ \cosh^2\left(\frac{a\tau}{c}\right) - \sinh^2\left(\frac{a\tau}{c}\right) \right] = \frac{c^4}{a^2} $$
This is the equation of a hyperbola. Thus, a uniformly accelerating observer's worldline traces a hyperbola in Minkowski spacetime.

**4. Rindler Coordinate Transformation**
We want to construct a coordinate system $(\eta, \xi)$ adapted to this observer. Let $\eta$ be the time coordinate (scaled to match the observer's proper time $\tau$) and $\xi$ be the spatial distance from the observer. 
We generalize the worldline equations to cover the spacetime wedge:
$$ ct = \left( \frac{c^2}{a} + \xi \right) \sinh\left(\frac{a\eta}{c}\right) $$
$$ x = \left( \frac{c^2}{a} + \xi \right) \cosh\left(\frac{a\eta}{c}\right) $$
Notice that at $\xi = 0$, we recover the observer's worldline, and $\eta = \tau$. 

Let's compute the metric in these new coordinates. Taking differentials:
$$ c dt = \left(1 + \frac{a\xi}{c^2}\right) c \cosh\left(\frac{a\eta}{c}\right) d\eta + \sinh\left(\frac{a\eta}{c}\right) d\xi $$
$$ dx = \left(1 + \frac{a\xi}{c^2}\right) c \sinh\left(\frac{a\eta}{c}\right) d\eta + \cosh\left(\frac{a\eta}{c}\right) d\xi $$
Substituting into $ds^2 = c^2 dt^2 - dx^2$ and simplifying using $\cosh^2 - \sinh^2 = 1$:
$$ ds^2 = \left(1 + \frac{a\xi}{c^2}\right)^2 c^2 d\eta^2 - d\xi^2 $$

**5. The Rindler Horizon**
The metric coefficient $g_{\eta\eta} = (1 + a\xi/c^2)^2 c^2$ vanishes when $\xi = -c^2/a$. 
*   **In Rindler coordinates:** The determinant of the metric goes to zero at $\xi = -c^2/a$. This is a coordinate singularity (much like $r=2GM$ in Schwarzschild coordinates), meaning the Rindler coordinates fail to cover the region beyond this boundary.
*   **In Minkowski coordinates:** Substituting $\xi = -c^2/a$ into our transformation gives $ct = 0$ and $x = 0$. However, looking at the hyperbola equation $x^2 - c^2 t^2 = (c^2/a + \xi)^2$, as $\xi \to -c^2/a$, we get $x^2 - c^2 t^2 = 0$, which means $x = \pm ct$. This defines the lightcone originating from the origin. Because $ds^2 = 0$ along $x = \pm ct$, the Rindler horizon is a true null surface in Minkowski spacetime.
