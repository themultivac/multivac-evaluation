Below is a compact but complete derivation of the Schwarzschild radius from the Einstein field equations under the stated assumptions.

---

# 1. Assumptions and setup

We assume:

1. **Spherical symmetry**
2. **Static spacetime** (time-independent, non-rotating)
3. **Vacuum outside the mass**, so
   \[
   T_{\mu\nu}=0
   \]
   and therefore Einstein’s equations reduce to
   \[
   R_{\mu\nu}=0
   \]
   since
   \[
   G_{\mu\nu}=R_{\mu\nu}-\frac12 g_{\mu\nu}R=0.
   \]

We seek the most general static, spherically symmetric line element. In standard coordinates \((t,r,\theta,\phi)\), it can be written as
\[
ds^2=-e^{2\Phi(r)}c^2dt^2+e^{2\Lambda(r)}dr^2+r^2d\theta^2+r^2\sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2.
\]

Here \(\Phi(r)\) and \(\Lambda(r)\) are functions to be determined from \(R_{\mu\nu}=0\).

---

# 2. Metric components

The metric is diagonal:
\[
g_{\mu\nu}=
\begin{pmatrix}
-e^{2\Phi}c^2 & 0 & 0 & 0\\
0 & e^{2\Lambda} & 0 & 0\\
0 & 0 & r^2 & 0\\
0 & 0 & 0 & r^2\sin^2\theta
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The inverse metric is
\[
g^{\mu\nu}=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\dfrac{e^{-2\Phi}}{c^2} & 0 & 0 & 0\\
0 & e^{-2\Lambda} & 0 & 0\\
0 & 0 & \dfrac1{r^2} & 0\\
0 & 0 & 0 & \dfrac1{r^2\sin^2\theta}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

---

# 3. Vacuum Einstein equations

For this metric, the nontrivial Ricci tensor components lead to the standard equations:

\[
R_{tt}=0,\qquad R_{rr}=0,\qquad R_{\theta\theta}=0
\]
(with \(R_{\phi\phi}=\sin^2\theta\,R_{\theta\theta}\)).

Evaluating these gives:

\[
R_{tt}=e^{2(\Phi-\Lambda)}c^2\left(\Phi''+\Phi'^2-\Phi'\Lambda'+\frac{2\Phi'}{r}\right)=0,
\]
\[
R_{rr}= -\Phi''-\Phi'^2+\Phi'\Lambda'+\frac{2\Lambda'}{r}=0,
\]
\[
R_{\theta\theta}=1-e^{-2\Lambda}\left(1-r\Lambda'+r\Phi'\right)=0.
\]

Primes denote derivatives with respect to \(r\).

---

# 4. First simplification: relation between \(\Phi\) and \(\Lambda\)

Add the \(R_{tt}=0\) and \(R_{rr}=0\) equations in a convenient way. The first has
\[
\Phi''+\Phi'^2-\Phi'\Lambda'+\frac{2\Phi'}{r}=0,
\]
and the second gives
\[
-\Phi''-\Phi'^2+\Phi'\Lambda'+\frac{2\Lambda'}{r}=0.
\]

Adding them:
\[
\frac{2}{r}(\Phi'+\Lambda')=0.
\]

Hence
\[
\Phi'+\Lambda'=0,
\]
so
\[
\Phi(r)=-\Lambda(r)+\text{const}.
\]

The constant can be absorbed into a rescaling of the time coordinate \(t\), so we take
\[
\Phi(r)=-\Lambda(r).
\]

Thus
\[
e^{2\Phi}=e^{-2\Lambda}.
\]

Let
\[
f(r)\equiv e^{2\Phi(r)}=e^{-2\Lambda(r)}.
\]

Then the metric becomes
\[
ds^2=-f(r)c^2dt^2+\frac{dr^2}{f(r)}+r^2d\Omega^2,
\]
where
\[
d\Omega^2=d\theta^2+\sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2.
\]

---

# 5. Solve for \(f(r)\)

Now use \(R_{\theta\theta}=0\):
\[
1-e^{-2\Lambda}\left(1-r\Lambda'+r\Phi'\right)=0.
\]

Since \(\Phi'=-\Lambda'\),
\[
1-r\Lambda'+r\Phi'=1-2r\Lambda'.
\]

Also
\[
e^{-2\Lambda}=f(r).
\]

So
\[
1-f(r)\left(1-2r\Lambda'\right)=0.
\]

Now relate \(\Lambda'\) to \(f\). Since
\[
f=e^{-2\Lambda},
\]
differentiate:
\[
f'=-2\Lambda' e^{-2\Lambda}=-2\Lambda' f.
\]
Hence
\[
\Lambda'=-\frac{f'}{2f}.
\]

Substitute into the \(R_{\theta\theta}=0\) equation:
\[
1-f\left(1-2r\left(-\frac{f'}{2f}\right)\right)=0
\]
\[
1-f\left(1+\frac{rf'}{f}\right)=0
\]
\[
1-f-rf'=0.
\]

Thus
\[
rf'+f=1.
\]

This is
\[
\frac{d}{dr}(rf)=1.
\]

Integrate:
\[
rf=r+C,
\]
where \(C\) is an integration constant. Therefore
\[
f(r)=1+\frac{C}{r}.
\]

So the metric is
\[
ds^2=-\left(1+\frac{C}{r}\right)c^2dt^2+\frac{dr^2}{1+\frac{C}{r}}+r^2d\Omega^2.
\]

---

# 6. Determine the constant using the Newtonian limit

Far from the mass, spacetime must approach flat Minkowski space, so
\[
f(r)\to 1 \quad \text{as } r\to\infty.
\]
That is already satisfied.

To identify \(C\), compare \(g_{tt}\) with the weak-field Newtonian limit:
\[
g_{tt}\approx -\left(1+\frac{2\phi_N}{c^2}\right),
\]
where the Newtonian potential outside a mass \(M\) is
\[
\phi_N(r)=-\frac{GM}{r}.
\]

From the metric,
\[
g_{tt}=-f(r)c^2dt^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{g_{tt}}{c^2}=-\left(1+\frac{C}{r}\right).
\]

So in weak field,
\[
1+\frac{C}{r}=1+\frac{2\phi_N}{c^2}
=1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}.
\]

Hence
\[
C=-\frac{2GM}{c^2}.
\]

Therefore
\[
f(r)=1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}.
\]

---

# 7. Schwarzschild metric

Substituting this into the line element gives the Schwarzschild solution:

\[
\boxed{
ds^2=
-\left(1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}\right)c^2dt^2
+\frac{dr^2}{1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}}
+r^2(d\theta^2+\sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2)
}
\]

This is the unique static, spherically symmetric vacuum solution: **Birkhoff’s theorem** guarantees this.

---

# 8. Identify the Schwarzschild radius

The metric coefficients become singular when
\[
1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}=0.
\]

Solving,
\[
r=\frac{2GM}{c^2}.
\]

This defines the **Schwarzschild radius**:
\[
\boxed{r_s=\frac{2GM}{c^2}}.
\]

At this radius:

- \(g_{tt}=0\)
- \(g_{rr}\to \infty\)

In Schwarzschild coordinates, this looks singular, but it is a **coordinate singularity**, not a physical curvature singularity. The true physical singularity is at
\[
r=0.
\]

Indeed, curvature invariants such as the Kretschmann scalar remain finite at \(r=r_s\) and diverge only at \(r=0\):
\[
K=R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}
=\frac{48G^2M^2}{c^4r^6}.
\]

At \(r=r_s\), this is finite; at \(r=0\), it diverges.

---

# 9. Physical interpretation

The surface \(r=r_s\) is the **event horizon** of a Schwarzschild black hole. In Schwarzschild coordinates, it appears as a singularity, but in regular coordinates (e.g. Eddington–Finkelstein or Kruskal–Szekeres), spacetime is smooth there.

Thus the radius at which the vacuum Schwarzschild metric develops its horizon is

\[
\boxed{r_s=\frac{2GM}{c^2}}.
\]

---

# Final result

Starting from the vacuum Einstein equations \(R_{\mu\nu}=0\) under the assumptions of static spherical symmetry, one obtains the Schwarzschild metric
\[
\boxed{
ds^2=
-\left(1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}\right)c^2dt^2
+\left(1-\frac{2GM}{c^2r}\right)^{-1}dr^2
+r^2d\Omega^2
}
\]
and the coordinate singularity occurs at
\[
\boxed{r_s=\frac{2GM}{c^2}}.
\]

That is the Schwarzschild radius.
