The Einstein field equations are
\[
R_{\mu\nu} - \frac12 R g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}.
\]
We assume a vanishing cosmological constant \(\Lambda = 0\). We seek a solution in vacuum (\(T_{\mu\nu} = 0\)) exterior to a spherically symmetric, nonrotating, uncharged mass \(M\). Vacuum implies \(R_{\mu\nu} = 0\) (tracing the equations yields \(R = 0\), so the Einstein tensor vanishes if and only if the Ricci tensor does). By Birkhoff's theorem, the vacuum solution is necessarily static (time-independent with no \(dt\,dr\) cross terms). Spherical symmetry further restricts the form of the line element to depend only on a radial coordinate \(r\), with the angular sector reducing to the standard round 2-sphere. Asymptotic flatness (Minkowski spacetime recovered as \(r \to \infty\)) is also assumed. Thus, the general ansatz is
\[
ds^2 = -A(r)\,c^2\,dt^2 + B(r)\,dr^2 + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2),
\]
where \(A(r) \to 1\) and \(B(r) \to 1\) as \(r \to \infty\).

The nonvanishing Christoffel symbols for this metric are
\[
\begin{align*}
\Gamma^t_{tr} &= \Gamma^t_{rt} = \frac{A'}{2A}, \\
\Gamma^r_{tt} &= \frac{c^2 A'}{2B}, \\
\Gamma^r_{rr} &= \frac{B'}{2B}, \\
\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta} &= -\frac{r}{B}, \\
\Gamma^r_{\phi\phi} &= -\frac{r\sin^2\theta}{B}, \\
\Gamma^\theta_{r\theta} &= \Gamma^\theta_{\theta r} = \frac{1}{r}, \\
\Gamma^\phi_{r\phi} &= \Gamma^\phi_{\phi r} = \frac{1}{r}, \\
\Gamma^\phi_{\theta\phi} &= \Gamma^\phi_{\phi\theta} = \cot\theta, \\
\Gamma^\theta_{\phi\phi} &= -\sin\theta\cos\theta,
\end{align*}
\]
with all others following by symmetry (primes denote \(d/dr\); symbols without explicit \(c\) factors arise from the angular sector).

The Ricci tensor is
\[
R_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\lambda\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\nu\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\lambda} + \Gamma^\lambda_{\sigma\lambda}\Gamma^\sigma_{\mu\nu} - \Gamma^\lambda_{\sigma\nu}\Gamma^\sigma_{\mu\lambda}.
\]
The Einstein equations reduce to the independent conditions \(R_{tt} = 0\), \(R_{rr} = 0\), and \(R_{\theta\theta} = 0\) (the \(\phi\phi\) component is proportional to the \(\theta\theta\) component by spherical symmetry; off-diagonal components such as \(R_{tr}\) vanish identically by staticity).

First consider \(R_{tr} = 0\). Substituting the Christoffel symbols yields
\[
\partial_r\left(\frac{A'}{A} + \frac{B'}{B}\right) = 0
\]
(after cancellation of lower-order terms). Integration gives \(A(r)B(r) = K\) (constant). Asymptotic flatness forces \(K = 1\), so
\[
B(r) = \frac{1}{A(r)}.
\]
The metric simplifies to
\[
ds^2 = -A(r)\,c^2\,dt^2 + \frac{dr^2}{A(r)} + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2).
\]

Next impose \(R_{\theta\theta} = 0\). Substituting all Christoffel symbols and collecting terms produces
\[
R_{\theta\theta} = 1 - \frac{1}{B} - \frac{r A'}{2AB} + \frac{r B'}{2B^2}.
\]
(The cancellations yielding this compact form include \(\csc^2\theta - \cot^2\theta = 1\) from angular Christoffel symbols, which ensures \(R_{\theta\theta} = 0\) holds identically for flat spacetime when \(A = B = 1\).) Setting \(R_{\theta\theta} = 0\) with \(B = 1/A\) (so \(B' = -A'/A^2\)) yields
\[
1 - A - r A' = 0.
\]
This is equivalent to
\[
\frac{dA}{1 - A} = \frac{dr}{r}.
\]
Direct integration produces
\[
-\ln|1 - A| = \ln r + C_1 \implies 1 - A = \frac{C}{r},
\]
or
\[
A(r) = 1 - \frac{C}{r},
\]
where \(C > 0\) is an integration constant (the sign ensures a sensible Newtonian limit below). Thus, the Schwarzschild metric is
\[
ds^2 = -\left(1 - \frac{C}{r}\right)c^2\,dt^2 + \frac{dr^2}{1 - C/r} + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2).
\]
(The remaining equation \(R_{tt} = 0\) or \(R_{rr} = 0\) is automatically satisfied by this form, as can be verified by direct substitution of the Christoffel symbols derived from it.)

To fix \(C\), match to the Newtonian limit. For weak fields (\(|\Phi| \ll c^2\), where \(\Phi\) is the Newtonian gravitational potential), \(g_{tt} \approx -(1 + 2\Phi/c^2)c^2\), so \(A(r) \approx 1 + 2\Phi/c^2\). Poisson's equation \(\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho\) for a spherical mass \(M\) has solution \(\Phi = -GM/r\). Thus,
\[
A(r) \approx 1 - \frac{2GM}{c^2 r} \implies C = \frac{2GM}{c^2}.
\]
The Schwarzschild metric is therefore
\[
ds^2 = -\left(1 - \frac{2GM}{c^2 r}\right)c^2\,dt^2 + \frac{dr^2}{1 - 2GM/(c^2 r)} + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2).
\]
A coordinate singularity occurs where the metric coefficients diverge or vanish, i.e., where \(A(r) = 0\) or equivalently where the coefficient of \(dr^2\) diverges. This is at
\[
r = r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}.
\]
(This is a coordinate singularity, not a curvature singularity: the Kretschmann scalar \(R_{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta}R^{\alpha\beta\gamma\delta}\) remains finite as \(r \to r_s\), and the metric can be extended smoothly across \(r_s\) in other coordinate systems, e.g., Eddington–Finkelstein or Kruskal–Szekeres.)

**Final Answer**

\[\frac{2GM}{c^{2}}\]
