# Derivation of the Schwarzschild Radius from the Einstein Field Equations

## 1. Starting Point: The Einstein Field Equations

The Einstein field equations (EFE) in vacuum (outside the mass distribution) are:

$$
R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2} g_{\mu\nu} R = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}
$$

In vacuum, $T_{\mu\nu} = 0$. Contracting with $g^{\mu\nu}$ gives $R = 0$, so the vacuum EFE reduce to:

$$
R_{\mu\nu} = 0.
$$

This is the equation we must solve.

## 2. Physical Assumptions

1. **Spherical symmetry**: the metric is invariant under spatial rotations.
2. **Static**: the metric is time-independent ($\partial_t g_{\mu\nu}=0$) and has no time-space cross terms (no rotation, no time-reversal-violating structure).
3. **Vacuum** outside the source: $T_{\mu\nu}=0$.
4. **Asymptotic flatness**: as $r\to\infty$, the metric reduces to Minkowski in spherical coordinates.
5. **Weak-field correspondence**: in the Newtonian limit, the metric reproduces $\Phi = -GM/r$.

## 3. General Form of the Static, Spherically Symmetric Metric

The most general line element consistent with assumptions (1) and (2) can be written (after a suitable radial coordinate redefinition so that $r$ is the areal radius, i.e. spheres of constant $r$ have area $4\pi r^2$) as:

$$
ds^2 = -e^{2\nu(r)}\,c^2 dt^2 + e^{2\lambda(r)}\,dr^2 + r^2\left(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\, d\phi^2\right).
$$

Here $\nu(r)$ and $\lambda(r)$ are two unknown functions to be determined. The metric components are:

$$
g_{tt}= -e^{2\nu}c^2, \quad g_{rr}=e^{2\lambda},\quad g_{\theta\theta}=r^2,\quad g_{\phi\phi}=r^2\sin^2\theta.
$$

## 4. Christoffel Symbols

Using $\Gamma^\alpha_{\mu\nu} = \tfrac{1}{2}g^{\alpha\beta}(\partial_\mu g_{\nu\beta}+\partial_\nu g_{\mu\beta}-\partial_\beta g_{\mu\nu})$, with primes denoting $d/dr$, the nonzero Christoffel symbols are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&\Gamma^t_{tr}=\nu',\quad \Gamma^r_{tt}=\nu' e^{2\nu-2\lambda}c^2,\quad \Gamma^r_{rr}=\lambda',\\
&\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta}=-r e^{-2\lambda},\quad \Gamma^r_{\phi\phi}=-r\sin^2\theta\, e^{-2\lambda},\\
&\Gamma^\theta_{r\theta}=\Gamma^\phi_{r\phi}=\frac{1}{r},\quad \Gamma^\theta_{\phi\phi}=-\sin\theta\cos\theta,\quad \Gamma^\phi_{\theta\phi}=\cot\theta.
\end{aligned}
$$

## 5. Ricci Tensor Components

Using $R_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\alpha\Gamma^\alpha_{\mu\nu}-\partial_\nu\Gamma^\alpha_{\mu\alpha}+\Gamma^\alpha_{\alpha\beta}\Gamma^\beta_{\mu\nu}-\Gamma^\alpha_{\nu\beta}\Gamma^\beta_{\mu\alpha}$, after a somewhat lengthy but standard computation:

$$
\begin{aligned}
R_{tt} &= e^{2\nu-2\lambda}c^2\left[\nu''+(\nu')^2-\nu'\lambda'+\frac{2\nu'}{r}\right],\\[4pt]
R_{rr} &= -\nu''-(\nu')^2+\nu'\lambda'+\frac{2\lambda'}{r},\\[4pt]
R_{\theta\theta} &= 1 - e^{-2\lambda}\left[1+r(\nu'-\lambda')\right],\\[4pt]
R_{\phi\phi} &= \sin^2\theta\, R_{\theta\theta}.
\end{aligned}
$$

## 6. Solving the Vacuum Field Equations

**Step A.** Consider the combination

$$
e^{2\lambda-2\nu}\frac{R_{tt}}{c^2}+R_{rr}=\frac{2(\nu'+\lambda')}{r}=0.
$$

Therefore $\nu'+\lambda'=0$, which integrates to $\nu+\lambda=\text{const}$. By asymptotic flatness ($\nu,\lambda\to 0$ at $r\to\infty$), the constant is zero:

$$
\boxed{\nu(r) = -\lambda(r)}.
$$

**Step B.** Use $R_{\theta\theta}=0$:

$$
1 = e^{-2\lambda}\left[1 + r(\nu'-\lambda')\right] = e^{2\nu}\left[1 + 2r\nu'\right],
$$

where we used $\lambda=-\nu$. Noting that $\dfrac{d}{dr}\!\left(r e^{2\nu}\right) = e^{2\nu}(1+2r\nu')$, this becomes

$$
\frac{d}{dr}\!\left(r e^{2\nu}\right)=1.
$$

Integrating:

$$
r\, e^{2\nu} = r + C \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e^{2\nu} = 1 + \frac{C}{r},
$$

with $C$ an integration constant.

**Step C.** One can verify that $R_{rr}=0$ and $R_{tt}=0$ are then automatically satisfied.

## 7. Fixing the Constant via the Newtonian Limit

In the weak-field, slow-motion limit, general relativity must reproduce Newtonian gravity, which requires:

$$
g_{tt} \approx -c^2\left(1+\frac{2\Phi}{c^2}\right),\qquad \Phi(r)=-\frac{GM}{r}.
$$

Thus

$$
e^{2\nu}=1+\frac{2\Phi}{c^2}=1-\frac{2GM}{c^2 r}.
$$

Comparing with $e^{2\nu}=1+C/r$ yields

$$
C = -\frac{2GM}{c^2}.
$$

## 8. The Schwarzschild Metric

Substituting back:

$$
\boxed{\,ds^2 = -\left(1-\frac{2GM}{c^2 r}\right)c^2\,dt^2 + \left(1-\frac{2GM}{c^2 r}\right)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2\left(d\theta^2+\sin^2\theta\, d\phi^2\right)\,}
$$

This is the unique spherically symmetric vacuum solution (Birkhoff's theorem guarantees uniqueness, even without the static assumption).

## 9. Identifying the Schwarzschild Radius

Inspect the metric coefficients. Two special radii appear:

- $r=0$: $g_{tt}\to-\infty$. Computing the curvature scalar
  $$R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}=\frac{48 G^2M^2}{c^4 r^6}$$
  shows this is a **genuine physical (curvature) singularity**.

- $r=r_s$ where $1-\dfrac{2GM}{c^2 r_s}=0$: $g_{rr}\to\infty$ and $g_{tt}\to 0$. The Kretschmann scalar is finite there, so this is a **coordinate singularity** (removable by, e.g., Kruskal–Szekeres coordinates). It defines the **event horizon**.

Setting $1-\dfrac{2GM}{c^2 r_s}=0$:

$$
\boxed{\; r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} \;}
$$

## 10. Physical Interpretation

- For $r>r_s$: the coordinate $t$ is timelike and $r$ spacelike; the exterior is static.
- For $r<r_s$: the signs of $g_{tt}$ and $g_{rr}$ flip—$r$ becomes timelike—so decreasing $r$ is an inevitable future evolution, not a spatial choice. Nothing, not even light, can escape.
- Numerically, $r_s \approx 2.95\text{ km}\times(M/M_\odot)$.

The Schwarzschild radius thus emerges as the location at which the coordinate chart breaks down and, physically, as the event horizon of a non-rotating, uncharged black hole of mass $M$.
