Below is a standard derivation of the Casimir force between two infinite, perfectly conducting, parallel plates separated by distance \(d\), starting from the zero-point energy of the quantized electromagnetic field.

I will use an exponential regulator and explicitly subtract the free-vacuum contribution. I will also state where standard analytic continuation results are invoked.

---

# 1. Physical setup and assumptions

We assume:

1. **Two infinite, perfectly conducting, parallel plates** lie at
   \[
   z=0,\qquad z=d.
   \]
2. The space between them is vacuum.
3. The plates are ideal conductors, so the tangential electric field vanishes on each plate:
   \[
   \mathbf E_\parallel = 0 \quad \text{at } z=0,d.
   \]
4. The field is quantized in the region between the plates.
5. We neglect edge effects because the plates are infinite.
6. We compute only the change in vacuum energy due to the plates; absolute vacuum energy is divergent and unobservable in this context.

---

# 2. Electromagnetic normal modes between the plates

For perfectly conducting plates, the allowed electromagnetic modes are standing waves in the \(z\)-direction and plane waves in the transverse \((x,y)\)-directions.

Let
\[
\mathbf k_\perp = (k_x,k_y),\qquad k_\perp = |\mathbf k_\perp|.
\]
The allowed longitudinal wave numbers are
\[
k_z = \frac{n\pi}{d},\qquad n=0,1,2,\dots
\]
with mode frequencies
\[
\omega_{n,\mathbf k_\perp} = c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+\left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)^2}.
\]

For the electromagnetic field:

- For \(n\ge 1\), there are **two polarizations** (TE and TM).
- For \(n=0\), only **one physical polarization** contributes.

A compact way to write the zero-point energy per unit area is therefore
\[
\frac{U(d)}{A}
=
\frac{\hbar}{2}
\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}
\left[
\omega_{0,\mathbf k_\perp}
+
2\sum_{n=1}^\infty \omega_{n,\mathbf k_\perp}
\right].
\]
This is formally divergent.

It is often convenient to rewrite this as
\[
\frac{U(d)}{A}
=
\hbar
\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}
\left[
\sum_{n=1}^\infty \omega_{n,\mathbf k_\perp}
+\frac12 \omega_{0,\mathbf k_\perp}
\right].
\]
But this still diverges and must be regularized.

---

# 3. Regularized zero-point energy

We introduce an **exponential cutoff**
\[
e^{-\varepsilon \omega},\qquad \varepsilon>0,
\]
which suppresses high-frequency modes. This is physically motivated as a UV regulator, and we will remove \(\varepsilon\to 0^+\) after subtraction of the free-vacuum contribution.

Thus
\[
\frac{U_\varepsilon(d)}{A}
=
\frac{\hbar}{2}
\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}
\left[
\omega_{0,\mathbf k_\perp}e^{-\varepsilon\omega_{0,\mathbf k_\perp}}
+
2\sum_{n=1}^\infty
\omega_{n,\mathbf k_\perp}e^{-\varepsilon\omega_{n,\mathbf k_\perp}}
\right].
\]

Since
\[
\omega_{n,\mathbf k_\perp}=c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+\left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)^2},
\]
this becomes
\[
\frac{U_\varepsilon(d)}{A}
=
\frac{\hbar c}{2}
\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}
\left[
k_\perp e^{-\varepsilon c k_\perp}
+
2\sum_{n=1}^\infty
\sqrt{k_\perp^2+\left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)^2}
\,e^{-\varepsilon c \sqrt{k_\perp^2+(n\pi/d)^2}}
\right].
\]

---

# 4. Free-vacuum contribution

Without plates, \(k_z\) is continuous. The vacuum zero-point energy per unit area in a slab of width \(d\) is
\[
\frac{U^{(0)}_\varepsilon(d)}{A}
=
\frac{\hbar}{2}
\cdot 2
\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}
\int_0^\infty \frac{dk_z}{\pi}
\,
c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+k_z^2}\,
e^{-\varepsilon c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+k_z^2}}.
\]
Explanation:

- There are 2 photon polarizations in free space.
- The density of states in \(k_z\) over an interval of length \(d\) is \(d/\pi\) for positive \(k_z\), but since I am expressing the energy **per unit area** for a slab of thickness \(d\), the correct replacement matching the plate mode sum is
  \[
  \sum_{n=0}^\infty{}' f\!\left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)
  \;\to\;
  \frac{d}{\pi}\int_0^\infty dk_z\, f(k_z),
  \]
  where the prime means the \(n=0\) term carries half weight.

Equivalently, the free-vacuum subtraction corresponding to the plate sum is
\[
\frac{U^{(0)}_\varepsilon(d)}{A}
=
\hbar
\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}
\frac{d}{\pi}
\int_0^\infty dk_z\,
c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+k_z^2}\,
e^{-\varepsilon c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+k_z^2}}.
\]

The Casimir energy is then
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas},\varepsilon}(d)}{A}
=
\frac{U_\varepsilon(d)}{A}
-
\frac{U^{(0)}_\varepsilon(d)}{A}.
\]

---

# 5. Rewrite in a form suitable for summation

Define
\[
F(x)
=
\int \frac{d^2 k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}\,
\hbar c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+x^2}\,
e^{-\varepsilon c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+x^2}}.
\]
Then
\[
\frac{U_\varepsilon(d)}{A}
=
\sum_{n=0}^\infty{}' F\!\left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right),
\]
where the prime means half weight for \(n=0\).

Similarly,
\[
\frac{U^{(0)}_\varepsilon(d)}{A}
=
\frac{d}{\pi}\int_0^\infty dx\,F(x).
\]

Therefore
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas},\varepsilon}(d)}{A}
=
\sum_{n=0}^\infty{}' F\!\left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)
-
\frac{d}{\pi}\int_0^\infty dx\,F(x).
\]

Now set
\[
a=\frac{\pi}{d},
\]
so that
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas},\varepsilon}(d)}{A}
=
\sum_{n=0}^\infty{}' F(an)
-
\frac{1}{a}\int_0^\infty dx\,F(x).
\]

To extract the finite part, use the **Euler–Maclaurin formula**:
\[
\sum_{n=0}^\infty{}' F(an)
=
\frac1a\int_0^\infty dx\,F(x)
-\frac{a^3}{720}F^{(3)}(0)+\cdots
\]
provided \(F\) is sufficiently smooth and the regulator is present. Thus
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas},\varepsilon}(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{a^3}{720}F^{(3)}(0)+\cdots
\]
and in the limit \(\varepsilon\to0^+\), the omitted terms vanish or correspond to divergent pieces canceled by subtraction. So the finite part is determined by \(F^{(3)}(0)\).

---

# 6. Compute \(F(x)\) near \(x=0\)

We first evaluate \(F(x)\) without yet removing the regulator:
\[
F(x)=
\hbar c
\int \frac{d^2k_\perp}{(2\pi)^2}
\sqrt{k_\perp^2+x^2}\,
e^{-\varepsilon c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+x^2}}.
\]
Using polar coordinates in the transverse plane,
\[
d^2k_\perp = 2\pi k_\perp\,dk_\perp,
\]
so
\[
F(x)=
\frac{\hbar c}{2\pi}
\int_0^\infty k_\perp\,dk_\perp\,
\sqrt{k_\perp^2+x^2}\,
e^{-\varepsilon c\sqrt{k_\perp^2+x^2}}.
\]

Let
\[
u=\sqrt{k_\perp^2+x^2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad k_\perp\,dk_\perp=u\,du.
\]
Then
\[
F(x)=
\frac{\hbar c}{2\pi}
\int_x^\infty u^2 e^{-\varepsilon c u}\,du.
\]

To determine the finite Casimir term, we only need the small-\(x\) behavior. As \(\varepsilon\to0\),
\[
\int_x^\infty u^2 e^{-\varepsilon c u}\,du
=
\text{(divergent terms independent of \(x\) or polynomial in \(x\))}
-\frac{x^3}{3}
+O(\varepsilon).
\]
So the \(x\)-dependent finite piece is
\[
F(x) \sim -\frac{\hbar c}{6\pi}x^3 + \text{(terms canceled by subtraction)}.
\]
Hence
\[
F^{(3)}(0) = -\frac{\hbar c}{\pi}.
\]

Therefore
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas}}(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{a^3}{720}\left(-\frac{\hbar c}{\pi}\right)
=
\frac{\hbar c\,a^3}{720\pi}.
\]
At this point the sign is clearly wrong compared to the known physical result, so I need to check the Euler–Maclaurin sign carefully.

For a half-weighted sum,
\[
\sum_{n=0}^\infty{}' F(an)
=
\frac1a\int_0^\infty F(x)\,dx
-\sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{B_{2m}}{(2m)!}a^{2m-1}F^{(2m-1)}(0).
\]
Since \(B_4=-1/30\),
\[
-\frac{B_4}{4!}a^3F^{(3)}(0)
=
-\left(-\frac{1}{30}\right)\frac{a^3}{24}F^{(3)}(0)
=
\frac{a^3}{720}F^{(3)}(0).
\]
Thus
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas}}(d)}{A}
=
\frac{a^3}{720}F^{(3)}(0)
=
\frac{a^3}{720}\left(-\frac{\hbar c}{\pi}\right)
=
-\frac{\hbar c\,a^3}{720\pi}.
\]
Now substituting \(a=\pi/d\),
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas}}(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{\hbar c}{720\pi}\left(\frac{\pi}{d}\right)^3
=
-\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{720\,d^3}.
\]

This is the standard Casimir energy per unit area.

---

# 7. Casimir force per unit area

The force per unit area is minus the derivative of the energy per unit area with respect to separation:
\[
\frac{F(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{d}{dd}\left(\frac{U_{\text{Cas}}(d)}{A}\right).
\]
Since
\[
\frac{U_{\text{Cas}}(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{720\,d^3},
\]
we get
\[
\frac{d}{dd}\left(\frac{U_{\text{Cas}}(d)}{A}\right)
=
-\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{720}\frac{d}{dd}(d^{-3})
=
-\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{720}(-3)d^{-4}
=
\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{240\,d^4}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\boxed{
\frac{F(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{240\,d^4}
}
\]
which is negative, meaning the force is **attractive**.

---

# 8. Final results

## Casimir energy per unit area
\[
\boxed{
\frac{U(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{720\,d^3}
}
\]

## Casimir force per unit area
\[
\boxed{
\frac{F(d)}{A}
=
-\frac{\pi^2\hbar c}{240\,d^4}
}
\]

---

# 9. Comments on regularization and known results

- I used an **exponential cutoff** \(e^{-\varepsilon\omega}\) to regulate the formally divergent zero-point sums/integrals.
- The subtraction of the **free-vacuum contribution** removes the divergent bulk vacuum energy, leaving a finite \(d\)-dependent remainder.
- I used the **Euler–Maclaurin formula** to extract the finite difference between the discrete mode sum and the continuum integral.
- No explicit zeta-function regularization was required in the main derivation.

A shorter alternative derivation uses the known analytically continued value
\[
\zeta(-3)=\frac{1}{120},
\]
which is a standard result from analytic continuation of the Riemann zeta function. If that route were used, I would state explicitly that this value is not obtained from the convergent series \(\sum n^3\), but by analytic continuation.
