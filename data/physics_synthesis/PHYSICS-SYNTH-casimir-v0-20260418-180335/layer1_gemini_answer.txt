Here is the complete derivation of the Casimir force per unit area between two parallel, perfectly conducting plates. 

### 1. Setup and Boundary Conditions of the Quantized EM Field

Consider two infinite, perfectly conducting plates placed parallel to each other in a vacuum. Let the plates lie in the $xy$-plane at $z = 0$ and $z = d$. 

For a perfectly conducting surface, the boundary conditions require that the tangential components of the electric field and the normal component of the magnetic field vanish at the boundaries:
$$ E_x(z=0, d) = E_y(z=0, d) = 0, \quad B_z(z=0, d) = 0 $$

These boundary conditions restrict the allowed wave vectors $\mathbf{k} = (k_x, k_y, k_z)$ of the electromagnetic field. In the $x$ and $y$ directions, the space is infinite, so $k_x$ and $k_y$ are continuous. We define the transverse wave vector as $\mathbf{k}_\parallel = (k_x, k_y)$ with magnitude $k_\parallel = \sqrt{k_x^2 + k_y^2}$. 

In the $z$-direction, the field must form standing waves, which quantizes the $z$-component of the wave vector:
$$ k_z = \frac{n\pi}{d}, \quad \text{where } n = 0, 1, 2, \dots $$

The angular frequency of each mode is given by the vacuum dispersion relation $\omega = c|\mathbf{k}|$:
$$ \omega_n(k_\parallel) = c \sqrt{k_\parallel^2 + \left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)^2} $$

For each mode with $n \geq 1$, there are two independent polarization states (Transverse Electric and Transverse Magnetic). However, for $n = 0$, $k_z = 0$, the electric field must be entirely perpendicular to the plates (in the $z$-direction) to satisfy the boundary conditions, leaving only **one** allowed polarization state.

### 2. The Formally Divergent Zero-Point Energy

In quantum electrodynamics, the vacuum state has a zero-point energy of $\frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega$ per mode. The total zero-point energy $E(d)$ is the sum over all allowed modes. 

Because the plates are infinite, we consider the energy per unit area, $\mathcal{E}(d) = E(d)/A$. We integrate over the continuous transverse wave vectors (with density of states $1/(2\pi)^2$) and sum over the discrete mode number $n$:
$$ \mathcal{E}(d) = \int \frac{d^2k_\parallel}{(2\pi)^2} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \! ' \, \left( 2 \times \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_n(k_\parallel) \right) $$
The prime on the summation $\sum'$ indicates that the $n=0$ term is multiplied by $\frac{1}{2}$ to account for its single polarization state. 

Converting the 2D integral over $\mathbf{k}_\parallel$ to polar coordinates ($d^2k_\parallel = 2\pi k_\parallel dk_\parallel$), we get:
$$ \mathcal{E}(d) = \frac{\hbar c}{2\pi} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \! ' \int_0^\infty k_\parallel \sqrt{k_\parallel^2 + \left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)^2} \, dk_\parallel $$
This integral diverges quartically as $k_\parallel \to \infty$. This is the infinite energy of the quantum vacuum.

### 3. Physical Regularization

To extract the physically meaningful part, we must regularize this divergence. We apply an **exponential cutoff regularization**. 

**Physical Assumption:** Real metals are not perfect conductors at all frequencies. At very high frequencies (e.g., X-rays, well above the metal's plasma frequency), the plates become transparent and no longer impose boundary conditions on the EM field. Therefore, high-frequency modes should not contribute to the force.

We introduce a cutoff function $e^{-\omega/\omega_c}$, where $\omega_c$ is a very large cutoff frequency. At the end of the calculation, we will take the limit $\omega_c \to \infty$. Let $\alpha = c/\omega_c$. The regularized energy per unit area is:
$$ \mathcal{E}_{\text{reg}}(d) = \frac{\hbar c}{2\pi} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \! ' \int_0^\infty k_\parallel \sqrt{k_\parallel^2 + \left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)^2} \, e^{-\alpha \sqrt{k_\parallel^2 + (n\pi/d)^2}} \, dk_\parallel $$
