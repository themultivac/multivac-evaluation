To derive the Casimir force per unit area between two parallel, perfectly conducting plates separated by a distance $d$, we follow the five steps outlined below.

### 1. Quantized EM Field and Boundary Conditions

We consider two infinite plates in the $xy$-plane at $z=0$ and $z=d$. For perfect conductors, the tangential electric field $\mathbf{E}_{\parallel}$ must vanish at the boundaries. The electromagnetic field is quantized into modes characterized by wave vectors $\mathbf{k} = (k_x, k_y, k_z)$.
*   **Transverse directions ($x, y$):** Continuous wave vectors $k_x, k_y$.
*   **Confined direction ($z$):** Boundary conditions require $k_z = n\pi/d$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, \dots$.

The frequency of each mode is $\omega_{n, \mathbf{k}_{\perp}} = c \sqrt{k_{\perp}^2 + (n\pi/d)^2}$, where $k_{\perp}^2 = k_x^2 + k_y^2$.

**Polarization Counting:**
*   For $n \ge 1$: There are two independent transverse polarizations (TE and TM).
*   For $n = 0$: The field is independent of $z$. The TE mode vanishes ($E_{\parallel}=0$), leaving only one polarization (TM).
*   **Zero-Point Energy:** $E_0 = \sum_{\text{modes}} \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega$.

### 2. Zero-Point Energy Density

The total zero-point energy $E(d)$ for area $A$ is:
$$E(d) = \frac{A \hbar c}{2} \left[ \int \frac{d^2k_{\perp}}{(2\pi)^2} k_{\perp} + 2 \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \int \frac{d^2k_{\perp}}{(2\pi)^2} \sqrt{k_{\perp}^2 + \left(\frac{n\pi}{d}\right)^2} \right]$$
Using polar coordinates $d^2k_{\perp} = 2\pi k_{\perp} dk_{\perp}$, we define the integral $f(n)$ for a single polarization:
$$f(n) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{\infty} k_{\perp} \sqrt{k_{\perp}^2 + \kappa_n^2} \, dk_{\perp}, \quad \text{where } \kappa_n = \frac{n\pi}{d}$$
The energy density per unit area $\mathcal{E}(d) = E(d)/A$ is:
$$\mathcal{E}(d) = \hbar c \left[ \frac{1}{2} f(0) + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f(n) \right]$$
This integral is quartically divergent.

### 3. Regularization

We apply an exponential cutoff $e^{-\lambda \omega}$ to model the physical reality that metals become transparent at high frequencies (plasma frequency).
$$f(n, \lambda) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{\infty} k_{\perp} \sqrt{k_{\perp}^2 + \kappa_n^2} e^{-\lambda \sqrt{k_{\perp}^2 + \kappa_n^2}} \, dk_{\perp}$$
Let $u = \sqrt{k_{\perp}^2 + \kappa_n^2}$. The integral becomes:
$$f(n, \lambda) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{\kappa_n}^{\infty} u^2 e^{-\lambda u} \, du = \frac{1}{2\pi} e^{-\lambda \kappa_n} \left( \frac{\kappa_n^2}{\lambda} + \frac{2\kappa_n}{\lambda^2} + \frac{2}{\lambda^3} \right)$$

### 4. Subtraction of Free-Vacuum Contribution

The free-vacuum energy density $\mathcal{E}_{\text{free}}$ is found by replacing the discrete sum over $n$ with an integral over $k_z$ (accounting for the density of states $dk_z/2\pi$ and two polarizations):
$$\mathcal{E}_{\text{free}} = \hbar c \int_0^{\infty} f(x, \lambda) \, dx, \quad \text{where } x = \frac{k_z d}{\pi}$$
The physically meaningful energy per unit area is the difference $\Delta \mathcal{E} = \mathcal{E}(d) - \mathcal{E}_{\text{free}}$:
$$\Delta \mathcal{E} = \hbar c \left[ \frac{1}{2} f(0) + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f(n) - \int_0^{\infty} f(x) \, dx \right]$$
Using the **Euler-Maclaurin summation formula**:
$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} f(n) \approx \int_0^{\infty} f(x) \, dx + \frac{1}{2}f(0) + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!} f^{(2k-1)}(0)$$
The difference simplifies to:
$$\Delta \mathcal{E} = -\hbar c \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{B_{2k}}{(2k)!} f^{(2k-1)}(0)$$
Evaluating the derivatives of $f(x)$ at $x=0$ via the Leibniz rule:
*   $f'(0) = 0$
*   $f'''(0) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \left( \frac{\pi}{d} \right)^3 \left[ \frac{d^3}{d\kappa^3} \int_{\kappa}^{\infty} u^2 e^{-\lambda u} du \right]_{\kappa=0} = -\frac{\pi^2}{d^3}$
Using $B_4 = -1/30$, the finite part (as $\lambda \to 0$) is:
$$\Delta \mathcal{E} = -\hbar c \left( \frac{B_4}{24} f'''(0) \right) = -\hbar c \left( \frac{-1/30}{24} \right) \left( -\frac{\pi^2}{d^3} \right) = -\frac{\pi^2 \hbar c}{720 d^3}$$

### 5. Force per Unit Area

The force per unit area $F/A$ is the negative derivative of the energy with respect to $d$:
$$\frac{F}{A} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial d} \left( -\frac{\pi^2 \hbar c}{720 d^3} \right) = -\frac{\pi^2 \hbar c}{240 d^4}$$

**Final Result:**
The Casimir force per unit area is:
$$\frac{F}{A} = -\frac{\pi^2 \hbar c}{240 d^4}$$
The negative sign indicates an attractive force.
