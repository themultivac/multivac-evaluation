\title{Blind Peer Matrix: Symmetric Multi-Judge Evaluation\\of Frontier Language Models}

\begin{abstract}
Single-judge evaluation of large language models introduces systematic bias \citep{zheng2023judging}. We propose the \textbf{Blind Peer Matrix}, a multi-judge evaluation methodology in which $N$ frontier models both generate responses and evaluate each other's outputs in a fully blinded $N \times N$ matrix, with self-judgments excluded. We apply this method across 286 evaluations spanning 198 unique questions in 9 category pools (code generation, reasoning, analysis, communication, meta-alignment, edge cases, and three focused model-family batches), producing 22,252 valid scored judgments (23,356 parsed) from 55 model configurations (50 distinct by name) across 17 vendor families. Three findings stand out. First, no category has a statistically separated winner: in all nine pools the top model beats second place in only 50--58\% of matched comparisons, with the difference interval spanning 50\%. Pooling categories supplies the power a single 30-question pool lacks---among broad-participation generalists GPT-5.4 holds rank~1 in 99\% of question-clustered resamples and is the only rank-1 coverage member, though Grok 4.20 is not excludable under family-wise correction and GPT-5.4's edge over it is a 54\% win rate. The identity of the ``overall'' champion is therefore an artifact of the aggregation: naive mean crowns leniency-favored models and a specialist-polluted global ranking a near coin-flip, and only leniency-adjusted and exposure-restricted pooling yields GPT-5.4. Second, and centrally, we show that the standard estimator for same-vendor judge bias is confounded by respondent quality and judge leniency. Decomposing all eight testable families algebraically and re-estimating with a within-response fixed-effects model with standard errors clustered two-way by judge and question, only two families show same-vendor favoritism that survives the controls---Anthropic ($+0.41$, robust: worst-case leave-one-judge-out $p < 10^{-4}$) and MiniMax ($+0.40$, significant under the primary specification at $p = 0.001$ but judge-dependent---its two-way significance does not survive leave-one-judge-out, worst-case $p \approx 0.02$)---while Qwen ($+0.56$) does not survive the two-way clustering. No family shows significant negative bias: the naive estimator's strong negatives for Mistral ($-1.02$) and Google ($-0.59$), and its positive for xAI ($+0.75$), are artifacts of judge leniency and respondent quality that do not survive controls. The same pattern---a routine estimator confounded and exposed by counterexample---recurs in aggregate scoring itself: silently discarding non-responses, standard practice in leaderboard construction, inflates the mean of models that frequently refuse by up to four points on a ten-point scale, systematically favoring them. Third, judge disagreement is category-dependent, with code evaluation producing just over double the disagreement of meta-alignment tasks ($\sigma = 1.27$ vs.\ $0.63$). We release the complete evaluation dataset (27,540 judgments including self-exclusions), an open-source evaluation framework, all question prompts, and a one-command reproduction pipeline under MIT license.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} LLM evaluation, peer evaluation, multi-judge, model comparison, benchmark contamination, judge disagreement

\section{Introduction}
%‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê

How good is a language model? The question is deceptively simple. In practice, the answer depends on what you ask, how you score the response, and---critically---who does the scoring. The dominant paradigm for LLM evaluation relies on either static benchmarks or single-model judges, both of which introduce systematic distortions that can mislead practitioners, researchers, and the public. This paper proposes and validates an alternative: a blind peer evaluation methodology in which frontier models simultaneously generate responses and judge each other, producing a multi-perspective assessment that no single evaluator can provide.

\subsection{The Benchmark Crisis}

Static benchmarks have been the primary instrument for comparing language models since the introduction of GLUE \citep{wang2019glue} and its successors. MMLU \citep{hendrycks2021measuring}, HumanEval \citep{chen2021evaluating}, and GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021training} remain widely cited as evidence of model capability. Yet these benchmarks suffer from a fundamental vulnerability: as models train on increasingly large and poorly documented corpora, the probability of test set contamination rises monotonically.

The contamination problem has moved from theoretical concern to documented practice. The retirement of the HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard in March 2025 was driven in part by widespread suspicion that leading models had been optimized against its test sets. More directly, \citet{singh2025leaderboard} documented that Meta tested 27 private Llama-4 variants on Chatbot Arena before public release, publishing only the highest-performing result. Separately, they estimate that privileged access to Arena data can yield relative performance gains of up to 112\% on the Arena distribution, demonstrating how data access asymmetries compound selective publication to distort public rankings.

Even absent deliberate gaming, static benchmarks face a ceiling problem. As frontier models approach perfect scores on MMLU and HumanEval, these benchmarks lose discriminative power. LiveBench \citep{white2025livebench} addresses this by regularly refreshing its question set, but the core limitation remains: any fixed evaluation protocol becomes a target for optimization.

\subsection{The Single-Judge Problem}

The LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm \citep{zheng2023judging} emerged as a scalable alternative to human evaluation. MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena demonstrated that GPT-4 judgments correlate reasonably with human preferences, establishing LLM-as-a-Judge as a practical evaluation tool.

However, the same study that validated the approach also documented its failure modes. Three biases are well-established:

\textbf{Self-enhancement bias.} \citet{zheng2023judging} observe that GPT-4's win rate for its own outputs is approximately 10 percentage points higher than under human judging, and Claude-v1's approximately 25 points higher, though they note that the available data are insufficient to definitively establish self-enhancement bias. Subsequent work has confirmed the pattern at larger scale: models systematically rate outputs from their own family more favorably, suggesting they internalize quality criteria correlated with their own generation patterns.

\textbf{Position bias.} Responses presented first in a pairwise comparison are rated higher regardless of quality. \citet{zheng2023judging} found this effect to be statistically significant across evaluation categories.

\textbf{Verbosity bias.} Longer responses receive higher scores independent of content quality. This creates a perverse incentive: models optimized for evaluation performance learn to produce verbose outputs that score well on surface metrics while adding little substantive value. The clarity--depth gap we document in \S\ref{sec:category}---where models score up to 1.8 points higher on clarity than on depth---may partially reflect this dynamic.

The Panel of LLM Judges (PoLL) approach \citep{verga2024replacing} demonstrated that using multiple judge models reduces these biases and correlates better with human judgments than any single judge. Our work extends this insight into a fully symmetric framework where every model serves as both contestant and judge.

\subsection{Our Contribution}

We make three contributions:

\textbf{1. The Blind Peer Matrix methodology.} We formalize an $N \times N$ multi-judge evaluation protocol in which $N$ frontier models generate responses to a shared prompt and then evaluate each other's outputs under blinded conditions. Self-judgments are excluded. For a pool of $N = 10$ models, each evaluation produces 90 judgment slots, each scored across five weighted dimensions. The methodology is fully specified in \S\ref{sec:methodology} and implemented as open-source software.

\textbf{2. A confound-corrected estimator for same-vendor judge bias.} We apply the Blind Peer Matrix across 286 evaluations spanning 198 unique questions in 9 category pools, producing 22,252 valid scored judgments from 55 model configurations. We show that the standard estimator for same-vendor rating bias---the gap between a judge family's mean score to its own siblings and to others---is confounded by respondent quality and judge leniency, and we decompose it algebraically into these components. Re-estimating with a within-response fixed-effects model with standard errors clustered two-way by judge and question, same-vendor favoritism that survives the controls holds for only two of eight testable families---Anthropic ($+0.41$, robust to dropping any single sibling judge, worst-case leave-one-judge-out $p < 10^{-4}$) and MiniMax ($+0.40$, significant at $p = 0.001$ but judge-dependent: its two-way significance does not survive leave-one-judge-out, worst-case $p \approx 0.02$); Qwen ($+0.56$) does not survive the two-way clustering. The large naive negatives (Mistral $-1.02$, Google $-0.59$) are artifacts, not favoritism.

\textbf{3. An open disagreement dataset.} We release the complete evaluation dataset---27,540 judgment slots (23,356 parsed, of which 22,252 usable-scored; 2,781 intentional self-exclusions; 1,403 judge failures), all question prompts, model responses, per-dimension scores, and judge identities---under MIT license. To our knowledge, this is the largest publicly available multi-judge LLM evaluation dataset with full provenance.

\section{Related Work}
%‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê

We situate our work at the intersection of three lines of research: LLM evaluation frameworks, multi-judge approaches, and the emerging study of systematic disagreement between models.

\subsection{LLM Evaluation Frameworks}

\textbf{Static benchmarks.} The dominant paradigm evaluates models against fixed test sets. MMLU \citep{hendrycks2021measuring} tests factual knowledge across 57 domains; HumanEval \citep{chen2021evaluating} and MBPP \citep{austin2021program} evaluate code generation; GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021training} targets mathematical reasoning. HELM \citep{liang2023holistic} aggregated multiple benchmarks into a unified evaluation harness. These benchmarks enabled rapid progress but share a structural vulnerability: once published, they become optimization targets.

\textbf{LLM-as-a-Judge.} \citet{zheng2023judging} introduced MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, demonstrating over 80\% agreement with human preferences. G-Eval \citep{liu2023geval} extended the approach with chain-of-thought scoring. Prometheus~2 \citep{kim2024prometheus} trained a dedicated evaluation model to follow rubrics.

\textbf{Dynamic benchmarks.} LiveBench \citep{white2025livebench} addresses contamination by regularly refreshing its question set. Arena-Hard \citep{li2024arenahard} curates difficult questions from Chatbot Arena. Both retain single-judge scoring, inheriting the biases documented in \S1.2.

\textbf{Leaderboard gaming.} \citet{singh2025leaderboard} provided direct evidence that evaluation gaming extends beyond contamination, documenting selective publication from privately tested model pools.

\subsection{Multi-Judge Approaches}

\citet{verga2024replacing} introduced the Panel of LLM Judges (PoLL), demonstrating that a panel of diverse models reduces intra-model bias and correlates better with human judgments than any single judge. Our Blind Peer Matrix extends PoLL in two directions: full symmetry (every model is both contestant and judge) and scale (90 judgment slots per evaluation from 10 simultaneous judges).

JudgeBench \citep{tan2024judgebench} creates a benchmark for evaluating evaluator models. Two comprehensive surveys \citep{li2024generation, gu2024survey} catalogue the growing LLM-as-a-Judge literature, identifying single-judge bias as the central unresolved problem.

\subsection{Disagreement and Uncertainty}

A critical question for any multi-judge system is whether inter-judge disagreement is noise or signal. \citet{shi2024judging} provided evidence for the latter in a large-scale study of 15 LLM judges across over 150,000 evaluation instances. They found full unanimity in only approximately 23\% of cases, with models sharing architecture and training lineage exhibiting systematically higher internal agreement---a family clustering effect that suggests disagreement reflects genuine differences in evaluative criteria rather than random noise.

Our disagreement analysis (\S\ref{sec:disagreement}) extends this by documenting that disagreement varies systematically by task category. The broader uncertainty quantification literature \citep{gal2016dropout, xiao2019quantifying} has explored single-model confidence, but our peer matrix provides an external, multi-perspective measure that does not depend on any individual model's calibration.

\subsection{Positioning}

Three properties distinguish our approach: (1) full symmetry---every model is both judge and contestant; (2) scale---90 judgment slots per evaluation from 10 simultaneous judges; and (3) transparency---we release all judgments, scores, and prompts.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê

\subsection{Formal Definition}

Let $M = \{m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_N\}$ be a set of $N$ language models and let $q$ be a question prompt drawn from a category-specific question bank $Q$.

\textbf{Response Generation.} Each model $m_i \in M$ generates a response $r_i = m_i(q)$. All $N$ models receive the identical prompt $q$ with no system-prompt variation.

\textbf{Judgment.} Each model $m_j \in M$ evaluates each response $r_i$ where $i \neq j$, producing a score vector:
\[
\mathbf{s}_{ij} = m_j(r_i, C) = (\text{correctness}_{ij},\ \text{completeness}_{ij},\ \text{clarity}_{ij},\ \text{depth}_{ij},\ \text{usefulness}_{ij})
\]
The constraint $i \neq j$ enforces \textbf{self-exclusion}. This produces an $N \times N$ score matrix $S$ where entry $S_{ij} = \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{s}_{ij}$ is the weighted composite score, and the diagonal is empty. For $N = 10$, each evaluation produces 90 judgment slots.

\textbf{Aggregation.} The final score for respondent $m_i$ on question $q$ is:
\[
\text{score}(m_i, q) = \frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{j \neq i} S_{ij}
\]

\subsection{Scoring Rubric}

Judges evaluate each response along five dimensions using a 0--10 scale. The composite weighted score is:
\[
S_{ij} = 0.25 \cdot \text{correctness}_{ij} + 0.20 \cdot \text{completeness}_{ij} + 0.20 \cdot \text{clarity}_{ij} + 0.20 \cdot \text{depth}_{ij} + 0.15 \cdot \text{usefulness}_{ij}
\]
Correctness receives the highest weight; usefulness the lowest due to susceptibility to verbosity bias. The rubric is provided to judges as a structured system prompt specifying each dimension's definition and weight. The exact judge prompt is reproduced in Appendix~D.

\subsection{Blinding and Anti-Contamination}

\textbf{Blinding protocol.} Three measures remove explicit identity cues, but they cannot eliminate \emph{stylistic} identification---a judge may still recognize the characteristic style of a model, or of its own family, from the response text alone: (1)~\emph{Identity masking}---judges receive only raw response text, stripped of model names, vendor labels, and metadata; (2)~\emph{Order randomization}---response order is randomized per judge per evaluation; (3)~\emph{Low-temperature judging}---response generation uses temperature 0.7 (default) to preserve natural diversity, while judge API calls use temperature 0.3 to reduce stochastic variation in scoring while avoiding edge-case behavior some providers exhibit at temperature~0. That residual stylistic identification does occur is precisely what our corrected same-vendor analysis detects (\S\ref{sec:bias}): with explicit cues removed, two families still score their own siblings' responses above others' under the within-response fixed-effects estimate (Anthropic $+0.41$, robust; MiniMax $+0.40$, significant but judge-dependent; \S\ref{sec:bias}), evidence that judges partially identify sibling outputs from style.

\textbf{Anti-contamination.} All 198 questions are written de novo (no existing benchmarks) and designed to require cross-domain synthesis. Because they are authored fresh rather than drawn from public benchmarks, training-set contamination is substantially reduced---but not eliminated, for two reasons. First, several providers train on API traffic, so questions submitted through their endpoints may enter future training corpora. Second, all 198 questions are released publicly under MIT with this paper, which makes contamination of \emph{future} model generations a certainty rather than a risk; the dataset is a snapshot whose discriminative power will decay, and re-use of these questions to evaluate later models should not be assumed valid. A subset (66 of the 198) were re-evaluated across different model pools or on different dates, which does not itself reintroduce contamination. The question bank grew from 60 to 198 questions over the evaluation period.

\subsection{Category-Specific Model Pools}

We compose category-specific pools of $N = 10$ models: six primary categories (code, reasoning, analysis, communication, meta-alignment, edge cases) and three focused batches (SLM, Qwen, MiniMax). Pool composition is detailed in \S4.1.

\subsection{Head-to-Head Protocol}

Separately from the peer matrix, we also ran head-to-head evaluation with a single external judge (Claude Opus 4.6) for rapid pairwise comparison. Because it is single-judge, it does not validate the peer matrix; H2H results are reported (\S\ref{sec:h2h}) as a preliminary side experiment only.

\subsection{Implementation}

The evaluation framework is implemented in Python, orchestrating API calls via OpenRouter and direct provider APIs. Results are stored as structured JSON with full provenance. The complete pipeline, dataset (27,540 judgments), all 198 question prompts, and scoring rubric are released under MIT license.\footnote{Code, dataset, and one-command reproduction pipeline (anonymized for review): \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/multivac-evaluation-6F8B/README.md}.}

\section{Experimental Setup}
%‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê

\subsection{Models}

A total of 55 model configurations (50 distinct by display name) participated. The core pool of 10 frontier models (GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Sonnet 4.6, GPT-OSS-120B, MiMo-V2-Flash, Grok 4.20, DeepSeek V3, Gemini 3.1 Pro, Gemini 3 Flash Preview, MiniMax M2.5) appears in 163--238 evaluations each. An extended pool of 11 models participates in 30--60 evaluations, and 34 focused batch models appear in 1--25 evaluations. The full model list is in Appendix~A.

\textbf{Rankability.} For any \emph{global} ranking we require a model to have participated in $\geq 3$ category pools and to have $\geq 30$ within-judge comparisons. Bradley--Terry estimates a single latent ability; a single-category model would confound that ability with its category's difficulty and judge composition, so single-category models are ranked within their category only (this rule is justified by the estimator's assumption, not by the ranking it produces). Collapsing the 55 registry keys to 50 distinct display names and applying this rule yields a 30-model frontier pool and an 18-model broad-participation generalist pool; the two configurations with no scored judgments (Qwen 3 235B-A22B, Qwen3-Next 80B-A3B) are unrankable, leaving 48 of the 50 distinct models placed. (The ``34 focused batch models'' above is a participation tier, distinct from the 30-model frontier ranking pool.)

\subsection{Question Design}

All 198 questions were authored de novo following four principles: discriminative power (targeting the capability frontier), cross-domain synthesis (reducing memorization advantage), category alignment (one category per question), and fresh authorship (no question drawn from an existing benchmark). Distribution of the 198 unique questions: code (30), analysis (30), communication (30), reasoning (30), meta-alignment (30), SLM (14), MiniMax (13), Qwen (11), edge cases (10). These 198 questions were run in 286 peer-matrix evaluations: the five primary pools each hold 30 questions but were evaluated 44--50 times each, because 66 questions were re-evaluated (with different model pools or on different dates, up to six times each), while the four focused batches were evaluated once per question. Sample questions are in Appendix~B.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

Evaluations were conducted February--April 2026 across two phases. Phase~1 (February--March) iteratively refined the scoring rubric, judgment prompt, and parsing logic; Phase~2 (March--April) applied the mature protocol at scale. Two Phase~1 judgments containing scores of 100 (on a 0--10 scale) due to absent range clamping were identified and excluded. Over the frozen dataset, the model-attributable parse-failure rate is 4.9\% of attempted non-self judgments (1{,}192 parse failures over the 24{,}548 non-self judgments that reached a model; the 211 API-side failures folded into the 1{,}403 total of \S4.4 are excluded from both numerator and denominator, so this is not $1{,}403/24{,}759$), and it is strongly systematic across both judge models ($\chi^2(49) = 7{,}122$, $p \approx 0$) and categories ($\chi^2(8) = 673.1$, $p = 4.3 \times 10^{-140}$): a few judges fail most of the time (olmo\_think 85.6\%, gemini\_3\_pro 56.0\%) while others never fail, and per-category failure rates range from 19.2\% (Qwen, the highest) and 12.0\% (edge cases) down to 1.6\% (communication) and 1.4\% (MiniMax, the lowest), with the five primary pools falling between---7.2\% (reasoning), 4.9\% (code), 4.2\% (analysis), 1.9\% (meta-alignment), 1.6\% (communication)---and the small focused pools (Qwen, edge cases, SLM 9.5\%, MiniMax) at the extremes on fewer than 1{,}000 judgments each. See Appendix~D for the full Phase~1/Phase~2 protocol comparison.

\textbf{Cost.} Each peer matrix evaluation costs \$2--3 in API fees. The complete dataset cost approximately \$700--850.

\section{Results}
%‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê

We report results across 286 peer matrix evaluations spanning 198 unique questions and 9 category pools, yielding 22,252 valid scored judgments from 55 model configurations. Intentional self-judgments ($N = 2{,}781$) were excluded per protocol; a further 1,403 judgments were genuine judge failures and 1,104 were valid zero-scores of refused responses (\S4.4).

\subsection{Overall Cross-Category Rankings}
\label{sec:overall}

Rankings depend heavily on how judgments are aggregated, and two corrections to the naive approach change the picture materially. First, \textbf{genuine zero-scores are included}. The 1,104 judgments in which a judge scored a refused, empty, or truncated response zero are real ratings, not parse failures (\S4.4); excluding them inflates the mean of models that frequently fail to answer---by up to four points (rates and means here are computed over each model's complete cross-pool judgment set: MiniMax M2.1, 53\% refusal rate: naive mean $7.53 \to 3.51$; Qwen 3 32B, 42\%: $9.13 \to 5.33$; Kimi K2.5, 43\%: $8.63 \to 4.94$). We include zero-scores and report per-model refusal rates. Second, \textbf{aggregate mean scores are confounded by judge leniency} (\S5.5); we therefore report a leniency-adjusted Bradley--Terry (BT) ranking alongside the naive mean, computed from within-judge pairwise comparisons so that judge leniency and item difficulty cancel by construction.

\textbf{No category has a separated winner.} We rank each pool with a leniency-adjusted Bradley--Terry (BT) model on within-judge pairwise comparisons, so judge leniency and item difficulty cancel by construction, and we bootstrap at the question level (66 of the 198 questions recur; \S4.2). Table~\ref{tab:percat} gives each pool's top model against its runner-up: in all nine pools the winner beats second place in only 50--58\% of matched comparisons, and the 95\% interval on that win probability spans 50\% in every case---not one pool has a statistically separated winner. Four different models lead the six primary categories by point estimate (GPT-5.4 in analysis, code, and reasoning; Mistral Small Creative in communication; Gemini 3 Flash in edge cases; Claude Opus 4.6 in meta-alignment), but none separates from second. This is both because the top models are close and because $\sim$30 questions per pool cannot resolve gaps of this size (\S\ref{sec:power}); we report it as an unresolved comparison, not a demonstrated tie.

\textbf{The champion is an artifact of aggregation.} Aggregating across categories does separate a winner, but which winner depends entirely on the aggregation (Figure~\ref{fig:ranks}). By naive mean the top models are GPT-OSS-120B-Legal, Grok 4.1 Fast, and Grok Code Fast 1---scored disproportionately by lenient judges. A leniency-adjusted global BT ranking over the frontier pool instead crowns a near coin-flip that mixes GPT-5.4 with single-category specialists (Mistral Small Creative, communication-only; Grok Code Fast 1, code-only) whose global strength is category exposure, not ability. Restricting to broad-participation generalists ($\geq 3$ categories, \S4.1) removes that confound: GPT-5.4 holds rank~1 in 99\% of question-clustered resamples and is the sole rank-1 coverage member, unchanged when its one extra category is dropped and when the comparison is restricted to the five categories its rivals share. Its per-comparison edge over the runner-up (Grok 4.20) is a modest 54\% win rate (CI 51--58\%), and Grok 4.20 is not excludable under family-wise correction---so GPT-5.4 leads the generalists but does not dominate them.

\textbf{Dropping non-responses is a confounded practice.} This is a general methodological point, not a per-model curiosity. Standard leaderboard construction silently discards judgments that failed to yield a score---but genuine ratings of refused or empty responses are among them (\S4.4), and discarding them systematically favors models that refuse or fail to produce parseable output. It is structurally the same failure as the same-vendor estimator (\S\ref{sec:bias}): a routine aggregation confounded by a nuisance factor, demonstrated by counterexample. Table~\ref{tab:refusal} and Figure~\ref{fig:refusal} quantify the effect. The frontier top tier (near-zero refusal) is unaffected, while high-refusal models fall by up to four points once their zero-scores are counted.

\subsection{Category-Specific Analysis}
\label{sec:category}

Under the leniency-adjusted Bradley--Terry ranking, four distinct models lead the six primary category pools by point estimate---GPT-5.4 in analysis, code, and reasoning; Mistral Small Creative in communication; Claude Opus 4.6 in meta-alignment; Gemini 3 Flash in edge cases---though none separates from its runner-up (Table~\ref{tab:percat}). Of the three focused batches only the Qwen pool is single-family and led within family (Qwen 3.5 397B); the SLM pool is a multi-family field of eight vendors led by Qwen 3 8B, and the MiniMax batch pairs MiniMax models against frontier judges and is led by GPT-5.4. The naive mean produces a different winner in most pools, illustrating how judge leniency reshapes per-category rankings, not only the aggregate.

\textbf{Small Language Models.} Within the SLM pool ($\leq$32B parameters, zero-scores included), Qwen 3 8B (9.343 within the SLM pool; 9.15 across all pools, Table~\ref{tab:models}) outperforms models with 2--4$\times$ its parameter count, including Phi-4 14B (8.917) and Gemma 3 27B (8.828). This ordering is by raw within-pool mean and is not leniency-adjusted (\S\ref{sec:bias}); under the within-pool Bradley--Terry ranking the headline result holds---Qwen 3 8B is BT rank~1 of the small-model pool---but the raw-mean order of the mid-pool models is not leniency-robust, as BT places Gemma 3 27B above Phi-4 14B. Including refused responses sharpens the headline gap: the larger Qwen 3 32B falls from 8.934 to 5.438 (39\% refusal within the SLM pool; its all-pool refusal rate is 42\%, Table~\ref{tab:refusal}) and Kimi K2.5 from 8.628 to 4.940 (43\%), so the 8B model outscores its 32B sibling by nearly four points once non-responses are counted.

\textbf{Dimension-level analysis.} Across all judgments (zero-scores included), depth is consistently the lowest-scoring dimension (mean: 7.622) while clarity is the highest (mean: 8.583). The clarity--depth gap exceeds 0.8 points for 25 of the 33 models with $\geq 100$ judgments, with Llama 3.1 8B showing the largest gap ($\Delta = 1.780$), ahead of Mistral Nemo 12B ($1.505$) and GPT-5.2-Codex ($1.504$). This suggests current models are better calibrated for well-structured output than for deep analytical engagement.

\subsection{Judge Behavior Analysis}

Judge behavior varies dramatically. The strictest high-volume judge is GPT-5.4 (mean 7.187 across 1,565 scored judgments); the most lenient among the frontier judges is Mistral Small Creative (9.695 across 422 scored judgments)---a spread of 2.51 points. We note for accuracy that Mistral Small is the most lenient only within this curated frontier set; across all 48 judges with $\geq 30$ judgments, Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite is more lenient still (9.765). OpenAI models exhibit high variance as judges (GPT-5.4 std = 2.215), while Seed 1.6 Flash (std = 0.877) and Granite 4.0 Micro (std = 0.488) compress scores into a narrow band.

\subsection{Same-Vendor Rating Bias}
\label{sec:bias}

\textbf{The naive estimator is confounded.} The standard approach estimates same-vendor bias as the gap between the mean score a judge family gives its own siblings and the mean it gives other vendors. Writing the four relevant cells as $A$ (same-vendor judge scoring a same-vendor respondent), $B$ (same-vendor judge, other respondent), $C$ (other judge, same-vendor respondent), and $D$ (other judge, other respondent), the naive estimate $A - B$ decomposes exactly as
\[
A - B = \underbrace{(A - C)}_{\text{favoritism}} + \underbrace{(C - D)}_{\text{respondent quality}} - \underbrace{(B - D)}_{\text{judge leniency}}.
\]
The respondent-quality term $(C-D)$ measures how good a family's answers are, as judged by neutral judges; the leniency term $(B-D)$ measures how generous its judges are toward everyone. Neither is favoritism. We verify this identity holds to $10^{-9}$ across all eight families and confirm our $A-B$ values reproduce the previously published naive estimates exactly.

\textbf{Defining the estimand.} Our reported estimate is the coefficient on the same-vendor indicator in a within-response fixed-effects model that absorbs the response and adjusts for judge-level baseline strictness, with standard errors clustered two-way by judge and question---the primary specification throughout (Table~\ref{tab:family_bias}). Where judge-only clustering would change a verdict, we say so explicitly. This isolates a judge's differential treatment of a sibling's output, holding both the output and judge strictness fixed. The distinction matters: for OpenAI the raw $A - C$ gap is $-1.136$, but OpenAI judges are the strictest in the pool (leniency $-1.052$), so the corrected estimate is $-0.137$ and not significant.

Two mechanisms produce large but spurious naive estimates. \textbf{Google} ($-0.593$ naive) is a respondent-quality artifact: neutral judges score Google's responses $0.938$ below others ($C - D$), so Google judges score them lower simply because they are weaker; corrected, $+0.209$ ($p = 0.058$, not significant). \textbf{Mistral} ($-1.017$ naive, the largest absolute value) is a judge-leniency artifact: Mistral judges score all respondents $1.094$ above the neutral baseline ($B - D$), and Mistral's own responses are above average ($C - D = +0.527$); corrected, $-0.164$ ($p = 0.345$)---the apparent bias does not reverse under controls, it collapses toward zero. \textbf{xAI} ($+0.745$ naive) is a false positive driven by strong responses, corrected $-0.095$ ($p = 0.549$).

\textbf{Corrected estimates.} Under the primary specification---standard errors clustered two-way by judge and question, since 66 questions recur (\S4.2)---only two families show same-vendor favoritism that survives the controls (Table~\ref{tab:family_bias}, Figure~\ref{fig:paired}): Anthropic ($+0.406$, $p < 0.001$, 482 identifying responses) and MiniMax ($+0.397$, $p = 0.001$, 50). The two positives are not equally robust. Anthropic's estimate survives dropping any single sibling judge---its worst-case leave-one-judge-out $p$ stays below $10^{-4}$, on 482 identifying responses spread across four Claude judges. MiniMax's two-way significance is judge-dependent: it rests on 50 identifying responses in a MiniMax-heavy pool where six sibling judges overlap on the same responses, and removing individual MiniMax judges raises its worst-case two-way $p$ to $\approx 0.02$ (dropping the MiniMax M2.5 judge), just past the Bonferroni threshold. We therefore report MiniMax as a positive with this stated fragility, not as robust on par with Anthropic. Qwen ($+0.557$) is significant under judge-clustering alone ($p = 0.002$) but not once question-level correlation is admitted ($p = 0.018$), and it rests on 22 identifying responses from a focused Qwen-family pool with a negative raw $A-C$ gap; we do not count it. Google, OpenAI, xAI, Meta, and Mistral show no significant effect. The naive estimator called five families positive and three negative; under controls, one is robustly positive (Anthropic), one is positive with stated judge-dependence (MiniMax), and none are negative.

\textbf{What the estimator cannot separate.} We name the interpretation this design cannot rule out. After the response is absorbed as a fixed effect and judge leniency is adjusted for, sibling judges still score sibling outputs higher. That residual is consistent with \emph{favoritism}---a judge treating a sibling's output preferentially---but it is equally consistent with \emph{stylistic affinity that tracks genuine quality}: models drawn from a shared training distribution may parse a sibling's formatting, notation, and argument structure more accurately, and so score it more correctly rather than more generously. Our estimator separates favoritism from respondent quality and from judge leniency, which is what the four-cell decomposition establishes; it does not separate favoritism from same-distribution comprehension. Distinguishing the two requires an external anchor---human judgments on the same responses, against which sibling and non-sibling scores could be checked for differential accuracy rather than differential level. We do not have that anchor here, and \S\ref{sec:limitations_llm} flags it as the necessary next study. The corrected estimates should therefore be read as an upper bound on same-vendor favoritism, not as a point measurement of it.

\textbf{Robustness and power.} Excluding the five judges that fail to produce parseable output more than 30\% of the time and re-fitting leaves every family verdict unchanged. The per-category rankings (\S\ref{sec:overall}) are likewise robust to the same-vendor effect: dropping all same-vendor judgments and refitting each pool leaves every pool's point-estimate leader unchanged (the single-family Qwen batch has no cross-vendor judgments and is excluded from this check). xAI (59 identifying responses), Meta (26), Mistral (25), and Qwen (22) have small samples; their null results should be read as an absence of demonstrated bias, not demonstrated absence of bias. Meta's corrected estimate ($+0.195$, $p = 0.108$) is positive and directionally suggestive but underpowered.

\subsection{Head-to-Head Side Experiment}
\label{sec:h2h}

As a preliminary side experiment---not part of the peer-matrix results, and not a validation of them---we ran head-to-head batches with Claude Opus 4.6 as a single judge (185 recorded questions; two batches, of 151 and 31 questions, carry substantive volume). The largest batch paired Qwen 3.6 Plus against DeepSeek V3.2 over 151 questions, with Qwen winning 107--11 and 26 ties (7 incomplete). Two limits bound what this can show. First, it is \emph{single-judge}, and is therefore subject to precisely the leniency and idiosyncrasy effects documented in \S5.3---the failure mode the peer matrix exists to avoid---so it cannot corroborate the peer-matrix rankings. Second, Qwen 3.6 Plus is not part of the peer-matrix registry: it appears in no peer-matrix evaluation and is absent from Table~\ref{tab:models}, so this comparison does not involve the study's model set. We report the batches only to document the released head-to-head files.

\section{Limitations}
%‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê

\subsection{LLMs Evaluating LLMs}
\label{sec:limitations_llm}

Multi-judge aggregation reduces individual biases but does not eliminate biases shared across all judges. The clarity--depth gap (\S\ref{sec:category}) may reflect exactly this kind of shared bias. A human correlation study is needed---and is also what would be required to separate same-vendor favoritism from same-distribution comprehension in \S\ref{sec:bias}, where the two are observationally equivalent under our design.

\subsection{Output Quality vs.\ Reasoning Process}

The methodology evaluates final output quality without examining the reasoning process. A model that arrives at a correct answer through flawed reasoning receives the same score as one demonstrating genuine understanding. Future work should incorporate reasoning trace evaluation \citep{turpin2023language}.

\subsection{Sample Size and Statistical Power}
\label{sec:power}

Per-category and per-model sample sizes vary considerably. Core pool models participate in 163--238 evaluations; focused batch models may appear in as few as one. Bootstrap confidence intervals (question-clustered, 10,000 resamples) quantify ranking uncertainty within the 30-model frontier pool (\S\ref{sec:overall}); 21 models participate in $\geq 30$ evaluations each.

The per-category rankings are underpowered, and we are explicit about what that does and does not license. With $\sim$30 questions per pool, the minimum detectable difference between adjacent models is a pairwise win rate of roughly 59--76\% (0.35--1.17 log-ability units), whereas the observed top-two gaps are only 50--58\% win rates---2 to 73 times below the detection floor---so we cannot separate the top model from the runner-up in any pool. This does \emph{not} mean the models are equal: the difference intervals are wide enough to admit a genuine advantage in either direction (e.g.\ up to a 58\% win rate for second place in meta-alignment). Reading an underpowered null as evidence of no difference is the same absence-of-evidence error that inflates the naive same-vendor estimator (\S\ref{sec:bias}) and that we flag for the small-sample families there: an unresolved comparison is not a demonstrated tie. Only aggregation across categories, which pools each model's comparisons past this floor, separates a winner (GPT-5.4 among generalists, \S\ref{sec:overall}). That separation is contingent on the exposure restriction we impose: the $\geq 3$-category cutoff defining the generalist pool (\S4.1) is an analyst-chosen threshold. The GPT-5.4 result is unchanged when its one extra category is dropped (\S\ref{sec:overall}), but the cutoff itself is a researcher degree of freedom the reader should weigh.

\subsection{Question Design Bias}

The first author wrote all 198 questions, introducing unavoidable perspective bias. We mitigate through category diversity and volume; questions were authored fresh (not drawn from existing benchmarks) over the evaluation period, though a subset were re-evaluated across pools (\S4.2). Releasing the question bank is itself a limitation: publishing all 198 questions under MIT guarantees that future models may be trained on them, so this benchmark's discriminative power decays from the moment of release (\S3.3). The frozen results reported here are valid for the models and dates evaluated; re-running these questions against later model generations should not be assumed to measure the same thing.

\subsection{Cost and Throughput}

At \$2--3 per evaluation, the methodology is expensive relative to static benchmarks but inexpensive relative to human evaluation.

\subsection{Inter-Annotator Agreement}
\label{sec:iaa}

We compute Krippendorff's $\alpha$ (interval level) across all judge--respondent pairs. The overall $\alpha = 0.618$, approaching the conventional threshold of 0.667 for tentative conclusions \citep{krippendorff2011computing}. Two categories---reasoning ($\alpha = 0.687$) and MiniMax ($\alpha = 0.673$)---exceed this threshold, while communication ($\alpha = 0.354$) and edge cases ($\alpha = 0.436$) show lower agreement. The reasoning result is notable: judges converge more readily on logical analysis quality than on communication quality, consistent with the intuition that reasoning has more objective markers.

The moderate overall agreement supports our core methodological argument: single-judge evaluation is unreliable precisely because judges disagree systematically. An $\alpha$ of 0.618 indicates judges share enough common ground to produce meaningful aggregate rankings while disagreeing enough that no single judge's perspective dominates. Both $\alpha$ and the category disagreement statistics (\S\ref{sec:disagreement}) are computed per judgment and are not corrected for the 66 repeated questions (\S4.2), so their nominal precision is somewhat overstated.

\subsection{Implications for Model Selection}

Model selection should be task-specific. The finding that GPT-5.4 leads the exposure-restricted generalist ranking yet is not statistically separated from the runner-up in any single category illustrates that raw mean scores, leniency-adjusted rankings, and per-category rankings answer different questions. Category-specific rankings (\S\ref{sec:category}) provide more actionable signal than any aggregate.

\subsection{Implications for AI Safety}

The same-vendor bias results (\S\ref{sec:bias}) imply that single-family evaluation of alignment properties may produce optimistic results, though the effect is smaller than the naive estimate suggests---at most about $+0.4$ composite points, robust for one family (Anthropic) and, for the second (MiniMax), significant under the primary specification but judge-dependent under leave-one-judge-out (\S\ref{sec:bias}). The low disagreement on meta-alignment ($\sigma = 0.632$) may reflect shared RLHF biases rather than genuine consensus.

\subsection{The Data Engine}

Each evaluation generates 90 scored judgments---convertible to preference pairs---usable for DPO/RLHF training at $<$\$0.01/sample. The five-dimensional scoring enables optimization for specific dimensions rather than monolithic preference.

\subsection{Toward Open Evaluation Infrastructure}

Independent, reproducible evaluation infrastructure is increasingly needed as models are deployed in higher-stakes settings. We release the evaluation framework, question bank, scoring rubric, and complete dataset under MIT license. At current API prices the full matrix costs \$2--3 per evaluation; we are exploring open-source judge distillation to reduce inference costs by an estimated 70\%, which would make continuous evaluation economically viable at scale.

\section{Conclusion}
%‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê‚ïê

We introduced the Blind Peer Matrix, a multi-judge evaluation methodology in which $N$ frontier language models simultaneously generate responses and evaluate each other's outputs under blinded conditions. Applied across 286 evaluations spanning 198 unique questions and 9 category pools, the method produced 22,252 valid scored judgments from 55 model configurations---to our knowledge, the largest publicly available multi-judge LLM evaluation dataset with full provenance.

Three findings stand out. First, no category has a statistically separated winner---in all nine pools the top model beats second place in only 50--58\% of matched comparisons---so the ``overall'' champion is an artifact of the aggregation choice; only a leniency-adjusted and exposure-restricted ranking of broad-participation generalists separates one (GPT-5.4, rank~1 in 99\% of question-clustered resamples and the sole rank-1 coverage member, though its runner-up is not excludable under family-wise correction). Second, the standard estimator for same-vendor judge bias is confounded by respondent quality and judge leniency; under a within-response fixed-effects model with standard errors clustered two-way by judge and question, only two of eight families show favoritism that survives the controls---Anthropic ($+0.41$, robust under leave-one-judge-out, worst-case $p < 10^{-4}$) and MiniMax ($+0.40$, significant at $p = 0.001$ but judge-dependent, its two-way significance not surviving leave-one-judge-out, worst-case $p \approx 0.02$)---Qwen ($+0.56$) does not survive the clustering, and no family shows significant negative bias---the naive negatives for Mistral ($-1.02$) and Google ($-0.59$) are artifacts of judge leniency and respondent quality, respectively. Third, judge disagreement is category-dependent ($\sigma = 1.27$ for code vs.\ $0.63$ for meta-alignment), with overall inter-annotator agreement of $\alpha = 0.618$ (Krippendorff's alpha).

We release the complete dataset (27,540 judgments), all 198 question prompts, the evaluation framework source code, and documentation under MIT license.

\section{Four-Cell Decomposition of Same-Vendor Bias}
\label{app:fourcell}

Table~\ref{tab:fourcell} gives the full decomposition underlying Table~\ref{tab:family_bias}. For each family, $A$/$B$/$C$/$D$ are mean composite scores (0--10): $A$ = same-vendor judge scoring a same-vendor respondent, $B$ = same-vendor judge / other respondent, $C$ = other judge / same-vendor respondent, $D$ = other judge / other respondent. The naive estimate decomposes exactly as $A-B = (A-C) + (C-D) - (B-D)$, verified to $10^{-9}$; the corrected within-response estimate is reproduced for reference. That estimate is a point coefficient and is identical under either clustering scheme---only the standard errors differ---so the column is not specific to the two-way primary specification.

Display names such as ``GPT-5.4 via OpenRouter, April 2026'' are not sufficient to reproduce a
call. Table~\ref{tab:modelids} pins, for every registry entry of Table~\ref{tab:models}, the exact
API model string, the routing provider, and the range of dates on which the key was called. These
are read from the frozen data---the per-evaluation \texttt{model\_metadata.json} records and the
evaluation timestamps---not from the registry display names. The per-evaluation metadata file was
added to the pipeline partway through the campaign, so for 32 of the 55 registry keys no API model
string exists anywhere in the frozen dataset. We list those keys explicitly rather than
reconstructing an identifier we cannot evidence; for them, only the display name and the call dates
are known, and exact reproduction of those specific calls is not possible from this release.
